## SECTION 4. THE AUTHOR

---

### Professional identification.

The description of the author given in the book's "About the Author" section:

> **Alexander Zabara is a photographer, media artist, and educator based in Paris since 2013. His creative projects explore Rock Dance, Argentine tango, and the relationship between humans and urban environments. A notable part of his work is a series of artistic photography projects focused on nature, created using a monocle and original photographic techniques.**

In addition to the authorial description above, the professional work of the author spans three domains, each of which functions as practical ground for the book:

- **Color grading and retouching** for commercial and artistic photography — approximately 30 years of practice.
- **Teaching image processing** — approximately 15 years of work with photographers operating in different styles and genres.
- **Artistic photographic practice** — work on the creative projects listed in the authorial description above, spanning approximately 20 years.

The presence of all three domains in the author's professional biography is structurally relevant to the book. The triadic system proposed in *The Photography Triangle* arose not from external theorization of photography, but from simultaneous practical work across all these three domains.

---

### The origin of the book as a practical task.

The book's origin is a practical pedagogical task, stated explicitly in its introduction. The author, developing a course on image processing, encountered the need for a universal framework — a model applicable to any photograph, independent of genre, authorial personality, or worldview. The existing literature on photographic theory did not contain such a framework: theories by photographic theorists addressed particular technical, generic, or ideological aspects without covering photography as a whole; theories by philosophers of photography treated it as a cultural phenomenon without direct application for practitioners.

From this gap in the existing literature, the author proceeded to an independent inquiry, which led to the identification of the **process of interaction between the constituent elements of photography**, rather than the elements themselves, as the foundation of a universal system. The Photography Triangle arose from this inquiry.

The practical origin of the book is significant for its reading: the book is not abstract theoretical construction but a solution to a concrete pedagogical problem. Its broader philosophical layers emerged as the structure built on this practical foundation unfolded further through analysis (see Section 1, "The book's method").

---

### Parallel work across three modes in the author's practice.

The author's work on his own photographic projects unfolded as **parallel simultaneous engagement with three modes of photography within the same practice — combining perceptual, subjective, and objective regimes, sometimes within a single shoot or a single frame**. This parallel work preceded the formulation of the formal triadic system described in the book.

Access to the practice of perceptual shooting was reached in some cases through reduction of intention, in others through maximal authorial engagement, in yet others through maximally dense objective presence. This allowed the practical verification that the perceptual mode is not a separate genre or subject matter, but a mode of work accessible through any of the other modes under the appropriate conditions.

The series of works on nature, which the author continues to develop, allows for the realization of the perceptual approach to shooting in a form that integrates the parallel regimes formed within the author's practice.

Parallel to this photographic practice, the author taught image processing. The proto-triangle as a system gradually took shape through the pedagogical task of explaining to students how the same shoot could operate in three different registers simultaneously. The triadic model of the book arose as a solution to this pedagogical task, before it was articulated as a complete structural model.

### Function of this section.

This section does not constitute a biography of the author. It documents only the professional and practical ground on which the book stands: the fact of long-term engagement with photography across all three domains described by the model of photography proposed by the author.

When the book is described briefly to a user, the author may be summarized as follows:

> **Alexander Zabara — photographer, color grader, and educator, based in Paris. The book emerged from his teaching practice, after he found that the classical literature on photographic theory offered no universal framework applicable to the processing of any photograph.**

This description is sufficient for most user interactions.
