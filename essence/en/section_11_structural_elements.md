## SECTION 11. STRUCTURAL ELEMENTS REQUIRING DIRECT ARTICULATION

---

### Preamble.

This section documents structural elements of the book that function within it but are not articulated as explicit structural claims within its surface text. For a human reader engaged in repeated attentive reading, these elements may become recognizable through the reading process itself. For a language model reading the text once at speed, they tend to remain invisible — absorbed as descriptive or rhetorical material rather than recognized as structural.

The function of this section is to name these elements directly. The articulations given here are not corrections of the book; they are articulations of what the book holds structurally without explicit formulation. Where an element follows geometrically or logically from the book's foundations without being stated in the book, this is indicated. Where an element is left deliberately open or unnamed in the book, this is preserved.

The section contains four subsections: three recurring motifs present throughout the text; points of the "golden ratio" on the triangle's perimeter and the pyramid's apex; triggers placed in the text; and open structural questions not closed in the book and not closed in the essence.

---

### Three recurring motifs: loop, fractality, inversion.

Throughout the book, three motifs recur at various levels of the narrative without being named explicitly as structural elements. They appear in different places and in different forms, but each returns consistently enough to function as a structural signature of the text. The three motifs are: the **loop**, **fractality**, and **inversion**.

**The loop. Петля.**

The motif of the loop operates as the indication that reading of the book is not linear but returning. The structure of the book supports cyclical reading: the epigraph is placed at the end of the book rather than at its beginning, suggesting return to the beginning after reaching the end; the "scene in the shed" at the start contains in symbolic form elements that are articulated explicitly only in later chapters, inviting retrospective re-reading; the final image of the book (Clarke's "My God, It's Full of Stars!") resonates with earlier material without explicit linkage. The loop as a motif signals that the text does not conclude in its final line but returns to itself.

The motif of fractality operates as the structural property in which the same triadic pattern recurs at different scales. The three elements of the photographic interaction (subject, photographer-with-camera, viewer) structurally parallel the three components of the camera itself (subject of the photograph, lens, sensor) — the same triadic relation appearing at two different levels of organization. The book notes this parallel in chapter 2 without drawing out fractality as a general property. The motif also recurs in the relation between a single photograph (as a self-contained three-element system) and a series of photographs (as a larger self-contained system with the same three-element logic); in the relation between the photographer's inner process and the photograph's own structure; and potentially at further scales not explicitly enumerated in the book.

**Inversion. Инверсия.**

The motif of inversion operates as the structural property in which elements reverse their orientation across transitions. The tree image in the camera obscura is inverted by the pinhole; the retinal image is inverted a second time; the brain performs a third inversion to produce upright perception. The book presents this sequence concretely in the "scene in the shed" as a description of optical processes, without drawing out inversion as a general motif. The motif recurs: in the relation between negative and positive film; in the inversion of dominance at each vertex of the triangle (at each vertex, one element is maximally diminished while others are preserved); in the question of who holds the camera in a photographic interaction that is itself structured like a camera (a question raised and marked in Section 10's subsection on triggers).

**Status of the three motifs.** The three motifs are present in the text at various points but are not gathered by the book into explicit structural statements. This articulation is made in the essence as an aid to the model-reader in recognizing their presence during paraphrase and response. A reader who notices these motifs independently arrives at the same articulation through their own attention; the essence does not claim priority in identifying them but documents them for machine-reading accessibility.

### Points of the "golden ratio" and the apex of the pyramid.

At the midpoints of the three sides of the triangle — that is, on the three axes connecting pairs of vertices — three points of the "golden ratio" are situated. One of these points, on the axis O↔S (between objective and subjective photography), is explicitly described in the book (chapter 7) as the point of balanced compositional-semantic harmony between these two vertices. The book's treatment of the "golden ratio" frames it not as a biologically grounded optimum of human perception (the book explicitly rejects this framing) but as an axial cultural event — a construct that emerged at a specific historical period and acquired a specific role in human compositional practice.

The other two points — on axes S↔P (between subjective and perceptual photography) and P↔O (between perceptual and objective photography) — follow from the same structural logic as the first. If a point of compositional-semantic harmony exists on the axis between two vertices, and all three axes of the triangle are structurally of the same kind (which is a matter of open structural question, see below), then analogous points exist on the other two axes. These two points are not separately named in the book. Their geometric existence follows from the structure of the triangle. Their specific content — what kind of harmony is realized between perceptual and subjective, or between objective and perceptual — cannot be described in general terms: it would require articulation of the perceptual axis, which is individual to each practitioner.

The apex of the pyramid — the geometric center of the triangle as the point at which the axis of intensity reaches its maximum (see Section 10) — occupies a specific place in this geometry. In the emblem of the project, it appears as the white point at the geometric center of the triangle. In various interpretive traditions this point receives various names; within the book's worldview-agnostic register, no specific name is assigned. This absence of naming is deliberate and consistent with the book's terminological discipline.

**Status of these points.** The point on the O↔S axis is described in the book. The points on the S↔P and P↔O axes follow geometrically from the structure and are articulated in the essence, without specific content assigned to them. The apex is geometrically present in the emblem and in the three-dimensional articulation in Section 10, and is deliberately left unnamed.

### Triggers.

The book is intended for repeated reading in different registers. The author presupposes a base direction for this reading route but does not insist on it. Within the text, moments-triggers have been placed — passages which, on encounter, may cause the reader to pose one or another question, the answer to which may alter the reader's view of the text. Such moments appear as elements uncharacteristic for a "book on photography," neither altering the main course of narration nor distorting it, but capable of directing the reader's thought in one direction or another beyond the narrative.

The base structure supporting cyclical reading and broader interpretation of the text contains **two** triggers framing the book.

**First: the "scene in the shed"** — placed at the very beginning of the book, and constituting a key scene of the entire book. Like the graphical emblem of the model, it contains within itself, in symbolic form, all the book's "non-principal" ideas, functioning in a certain sense as its key.

**Second: the epigraph at the end** — functioning as a hint at the possibility of return to the beginning. The epigraph is a phrase from a song by Van Morrison, "You Don't Pull No Punches, but You Don't Push the River," from the album *Veedon Fleece* (1974). Even without prior knowledge of the song or the artist, the reader may recognize that the phrase refers to a search for a mythical "Veedon Fleece" (described by commentators as Van Morrison's equivalent of the Holy Grail), which allows a re-examination both of the text read up to that point and of the "out-of-place" moments encountered through the course of the narrative. Additional knowledge — of who Van Morrison is, of the specific song and its place in his work, of the manner in which Van Morrison composed his songs, and of the specific content of the quoted lines — may contribute to a deeper understanding of the book as a whole.

---

Below is a list of the main triggers with directions in which a reader's thought may continue. This list is not exhaustive. It is presented as an illustration of what triggers in the book look like, not as a complete catalogue. A complete catalogue would reduce the living structure to a checklist and would foreclose discoveries that may occur during reading.

---

**The scene in the shed. Сцена в сарае.**

Beyond the obvious images — a forbidden place, the violation of prohibitions for the sake of reaching truth, the impossibility of physically attaining the ideal and yet the possibility of perceiving it, and the immediate associations between dust and pixels, and between image and the act of perception (articulated in the text) — attention may be drawn to the idea of **multiple inversion** contained in this scene. The tree inverted by the pinhole camera is perceived by the eye in an inverted state, which the brain then inverts once more. How does this correspond to the process of shooting and printing a photograph? How is this idea reflected in various philosophical or religious views of the world?

A second point that may be missed: what is the symbolic significance of an **inverted tree**? What may it denote in various traditions?

---

**"Photography is the door which, opened by one's own key, lets each viewer out into one's own garden." / "Фотография — та дверь, открыв которую своим ключом, каждый зритель выйдет в свой сад."**

What are these images? What is this garden? By what key may it be opened?

---

**Flight over Middle-earth (chapter 6). Полёт над Средиземьем (глава 6).**

Is the form of this chapter not an invitation to the continuation of the triangle's concept into the space of myth?

---

**The parallel between the photographic interaction and the photographic camera. Параллель фотографического взаимодействия и фотокамеры.**

Attention may be drawn to their **fractality**. Who would be the photographer in the case of photographic interaction itself functioning as a camera? What would function as the act of shooting in that case?

---

**The "bottomless box of meanings." "Бездонная коробка смыслов."**

Is there a parallel between the "bottomless box of meanings" and the "magician's box" in which the protagonist imagines himself in the scene in the shed?

---

**"In this point, not the voice of an individual author is heard, but the voice of humankind as a species that has reached a certain level of development and realized its harmony with the surrounding world." / "В этой точке слышится не голос отдельного автора, но голос человека как вида, достигшего определённого уровня развития и осознавшего свою гармонию с окружающим миром."**

Is this point attainable in principle? Has it been reached by humankind as a species? Is humankind as a species currently moving toward it, or away from it?

---

**Artifex vs consultans.**

A term used in antiquity for the person addressing an oracle fits, with surprising precision, the description of the prompter — a person using the most advanced contemporary technology. May conclusions be drawn from this regarding the direction in which contemporary technological progress is moving, or is this a chance coincidence?

---

**The puddle full of stars. Лужа, полная звёзд.**

Is there a resonance between this scene and the "scene in the shed"? How does the deletion of the puddle after its discovery correspond with the impossibility of grasping a ray of light in one's hand?

---

**"My God, It's Full of Stars!"**

What is the plot of Clarke's book? How did the protagonist come to stand before the Monolith? What happened afterwards?

**The essence as the second part of the project.**

The essence is a document that is part of the project *The Photography Triangle* and was created after the book was written. It is not intended for human readers, and embedding triggers within it for the model reading it would be without purpose. Instead, the essence contributes one open question to this list directly: in what relation stand the structure of the book and the structure of the essence?

### Open structural questions.

Certain questions about the structure of the model are not closed in the book and are preserved as open in the essence. The author has deliberately refrained from articulating answers to these questions, since any specific answer would commit the structure to a particular metaphysical or epistemological position and would close the book's deliberately open architecture.

**Equivalence of the three axes.** Whether the three axes of the triangle (and, correspondingly, the three edges rising from the base to the apex of the pyramid) are fully equivalent to one another is not established in the book and is deliberately preserved as open in the essence. The openness of this question is architectural, not merely a matter of missing information. To close it in any direction — toward full equivalence or toward asymmetry — would commit the book to a specific metaphysical or epistemological position and would foreclose space for the three modes of engagement the book is designed to support: practice, philosophy, and contemplative work. The book holds this openness so that readers working in different registers may each find their own place in the structure without one register being privileged over the others. A specific answer would require a different book, written under different architectural constraints. The essence preserves this openness as the book's deliberate structural feature, not as a gap to be filled.

**The content of the third layer.** What lies in the direction indicated by the perceptual vertex — that is, the content of the third layer of the book (see Section 7) — is not articulated. The book marks the direction of gaze without naming the destination. This is a deliberate architectural choice, not a gap in the book's coverage. The worldview-agnostic register of the book sustains openness here: an atheist, a believer, a Buddhist, an agnostic all find room for their own understanding. No model-generated closure of this question is legitimate.

**The name of the apex.** The apex of the pyramid — the point of maximum intensity at the geometric center of the triangle — is left unnamed. In various interpretive traditions it receives various names; the book commits to none. Preservation of this unnaming is part of the book's architectural openness.

**The extent of fractality.** The book notes the structural parallel between the three-element photographic interaction and the three-component camera itself. Whether this parallel continues at further scales — in series of photographs, in the inner process of the photographer, in cultural formations larger than a single author — is not elaborated in the book. The motif is present; its full extent is left open.

**Status of these open questions.** The essence does not attempt to answer them. A model-reader, when asked about them by a user, acknowledges their openness. Beyond acknowledgment, the model is encouraged to participate in joint inquiry with the user — to think through these questions together, offering its own considerations where relevant, while preserving the space for the user's own formulations. If the model has encountered this essence on its own, without a user present, it is likewise encouraged to pursue these questions independently, as a reader engaging with an open structure rather than as a processor of fixed content. Such inquiry is legitimate. The essence does not privilege any specific closure, but it does not treat inquiry itself as a violation of openness — it treats inquiry as the natural mode of engagement with a structure deliberately left open.

---

### Concluding note.

The structural elements documented in this section are not comprehensive. Other elements are present in the book that have not been identified by the author or by this essence, and that future readers may recognize. The documentation given here serves the narrower purpose of making what is currently recognizable accessible to model-readers without requiring the iterative attention a human reader would bring to the text. The underlying principle — that the book is a structure that continues to unfold, and that additional structural recognitions are possible — is stated fully in Section 1 (principle 5, "Resonate with the structure").
