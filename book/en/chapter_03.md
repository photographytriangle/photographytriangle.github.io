Chapter 3: Objective Photography

Chapter 3

Objective Photography

Another benefit of this analogy between the photographic interaction model and color space is the possibility of its visualisation in the form of a simple diagram.

Imagine a triangle whose corners are colored in the most intensely saturated primary colors—red, green, and blue. These very primary colors will be encountered by you extremely rarely. But only by describing them can you obtain, through their mixture and attenuation, all the other colors that surround you in everyday life.

Our photographic interaction model can similarly be visualized as such a triangle. Its corners will likewise be occupied by certain extreme states, which very rarely encounter us in their pure form in actual practice. Yet their understanding is absolutely necessary so that we can determine where between them an actual photograph will occupy its position.

You should understand that the analogy I am employing between color space and photographic interaction is quite conditional. And their extreme points will be structured quite differently.

As we established in the previous chapter, we cannot fully eliminate any of the three fundamental elements of photographic interaction. Instead, to achieve such extreme states, one of them must be maximally diminished.

Let us examine the first of these extreme states. Throughout this book, we shall refer to it, by convention, as objective photography.

In objective photography, the role of the photographer and their camera is reduced to a minimum. Such a photograph will convey the subject as closely as possible to what a viewer could see, standing in the photographer's position with a camera.

I would position objective photography at the top corner of our triangle, to underscore its significance.

And if I had to choose a color for its representation, I would choose green. It is in the green part of the spectrum that our vision is most sensitive to tonal nuances, and it is green that contributes most substantially to our color perception.

Most photographs we encounter will strive toward this vertex. This is precisely why we must comprehend in detail how such an image will appear in its extreme form.

I find it important to draw your attention to several characteristics of objective photography.

First, maximum approximation to what a viewer would see will not be identical to the actual process of their visual perception. There is no way to overcome the limitations imposed by the photograph itself.

The image will be two-dimensional; the frame will constrain the visible field; we will perceive only a single frozen instant; we will be unable to sense the warmth, scent, or sound emanating from the subject. Objective photography is the most accurate reflection of reality, but within the limits that photography allows.

Second, one must understand the difference between the image that falls on the viewer's retina and the image that arises in their brain.

The static image formed by the eye on the retina is highly imperfect. Our zone of sharp vision is extraordinarily narrow. But we do not notice this because looking at an object is not a single photograph, but rather a fragment of video. We usually do not even realize that the eye rapidly “scans” the scene before us, focusing on different elements of it. From these fragments, the brain assembles a composite image.

Moreover, part of the familiar objects we believe we have seen, the brain reconstructs from short-term memory or life experience. We look with our eyes, but we see with our brain.

Third, this image created by the brain will be perceived differently by each viewer, depending on their life experience, knowledge, and context. Perception of a photograph is an active process of constructing reality, not merely passive “reception of information.”

Objective photography is the creation of the most accurate possible “copy of reality” for subsequent perception by the viewer. And the aforementioned reasons impose rather strict limitations on such an image.

Before drawing any conclusions about objective photography, I propose that we examine the primary parameters of such an image. I will present their description not as a guide for adjusting your photographs, but so that you better understand what such an image looks like.

But at this moment I ask you to actively engage with the reading. After reading each point, ask yourself: how far are your photographs from this “maximum possible truth”? And can you answer yourself the question “why?” So:

Lens Focal Length: The human angle of sharp vision is approximately 40–60°. This corresponds to lenses with a focal length of 43 to 50mm on a full-frame sensor. A 50mm lens is commonly called a “normal lens”.

Aspect Ratio: Research shows that aspect ratios between 3:2 and 4:3 are closest to human vision. The 3:2 aspect ratio, common in digital SLRs and mirrorless cameras, is somewhat wider than human perception, while the 4:3 aspect ratio, more frequently found in medium-format and compact cameras, corresponds to it most closely.

Horizon Line: Always parallel to the top and bottom of the frame. Our brain forms and maintains a long-term internal model of space in which up and down are defined independently of our body's position. The horizon remains horizontal for us, even if we lie on our side.

Camera Position and Viewing Angle: They are rigidly determined by the position of the viewer's eyes relative to the subject. In objective photography, the use of camera angle as a creative technique is inadmissible.

Chromatic and Spherical Aberrations: Like any individual optical characteristics of a lens, these must either be absent or corrected during post-processing in objective photography.

Depth of Field: Maximum possible. Our brain actively suppresses the perception of blur, reconstructing familiar objects from memory or lived experience. Although we do perceive some unfamiliar or unrecognized objects as blurred, their placement bears no relation to the lens's focal point. Thus, overall maximum sharpness across the entire frame, while not identical to what the viewer perceives, comes closest to it.

Frame Composition: The primary subject should be located at the center of the frame. This most accurately conveys the alignment between head direction and gaze direction. This alignment evolved to compensate for energy expenditure, coordinate different sensory inputs, and accommodate our narrow angle of sharp vision. If the position of a new object of attention differs by as little as 15–25 degrees from the previous one, we automatically turn our head and look at it directly.

White Balance: In objective photography, it is corrected based on the context of the photograph and the duration of the viewer's contact with the subject. Our brain adapts to the color of illumination at approximately the same rate, regardless of its hue.

When lighting changes, the brain compensates halfway within 40–100 milliseconds. However, complete adaptation takes more than a minute, which must be considered when adjusting the photograph.

For example, lighting indoors with incandescent bulbs, which we experience for an entire evening, will be perceived by us as neutral. But a passerby glancing at it through a window will see it flooded with saturated yellow-orange light. This occurs because of the brief perception time and the bluish evening light to which the passerby's vision has already adapted.

Use of white balance alteration as a creative technique in objective photography is likewise inadmissible.

Color Profile: Realistic, constructed on the basis of a photograph of a standard color reference chart to compensate for the camera's and lighting's particular characteristics.

Distortion of color or saturation (as well as any other parameters) for the sake of greater expressiveness is not permitted in objective photography.

Dynamic Range: Maximum possible. Scenes in the world around us can span a range exceeding 26 exposure stops.

According to research, our eye, depending on conditions, can instantaneously perceive between 6.5 and 14 stops. However, as the eye scans across a scene, it gradually adapts the pupil size, and the brain combines details perceived during different fixations into a single image with a range exceeding 20 stops.

Unfortunately, here we encounter the imperfection of technology and the impossibility of reconciling the last two listed parameters in actual photography—and here is why.

We can create an image with an extended dynamic range, or HDR, by blending photographs taken at different exposures. A precise camera color profile is created from a single RAW image and can only be applied to another single image.

Building a color profile based on a standard color reference chart in HDR is impossible, since in the different exposures the color samples are either underexposed or overexposed.

This means we must choose which parameter to sacrifice.

Either we create an HDR image whose colors will not accurately correspond to reality. Though we can approximate the desired colors through manual correction, they will nevertheless remain “fabricated”, which contradicts the ideal of objective photography.

Or we create a single image with a precise color profile. However, the dynamic range of RAW files from modern cameras is 12–15 stops, which will be insufficient to capture scenes with highly contrasty lighting.

Or we create a series of single images with color profiles and combine portions of them into a unified image in a graphics editor. But this would mean our active creative participation in constructing the image, which would, once again, distance us from objective photography.

Perhaps technological advances will overcome these limitations, but for now they must be taken into account.

I believe that even this enumeration of the most fundamental parameters of objective photography allows you to imagine such an image. And I would encourage you, at least once in your life, to independently attempt creating an image as close to it as possible.

Examine it closely, listening to yourself. What do you feel in this moment?

Are you bored?

Does your gaze lack movement?

Does the frame composition fail to suggest a direction, leaving you feeling irritated?

Do you crave more color, contrast, accents? Intense emotions and experiences?

Does your sense of beauty demand a more aesthetic composition?

Does the unfamiliar silence and the absence of any coherent narrative trouble you?

Does the photograph fail to entertain you with unusual angles and authorial discoveries?

Viewing such an image is as strange and unfamiliar as catching your reflection on a cold Monday morning—or hearing your own voice amplified through speakers in a large hall.

With the photographer and camera having nearly vanished in objective photography, the viewer's voice begins to sound loudly and clearly—in this case, your own voice.

No other form of photography, as you will discover later, allows you to manifest your own beliefs, aspirations, and desires in such measure. To reveal them and make a decision about what you wish to photograph next.

You will not create such images except for purposes of self-analysis.

In reality, no photograph can exist that fully corresponds to all the requirements of objective photography. It is merely a theoretical extreme point on the Photography Triangle. Even a passport photo would be more conventional and expressive.

Such an image could be taken by you, me, or anyone else—there is no personal signature of the author in it.

You will forget this photograph the moment you stop looking at it. Only the image of the object or scene will remain in your mind.

Like distilled water, it is extremely pure, but it has no flavor and will not quench your thirst for beauty.

Objective photography is not an ideal to which you will aspire. It is a zero point of reference—a baseline from which you can push off to create something new, to tell stories, and to embody in your photographs what you wish to convey.

It will depend solely on you how far from it you venture—but like a lighthouse, it is always useful to keep it in your field of vision. Should you face the task of conveying objective reality, the parameters of your image that were listed above will gradually shift toward this “zero point of reference.”

Likewise, you can measure any photograph against the objective angle of the triangle by assessing how closely it approaches its “maximum possible realism.”

This analysis becomes particularly intriguing when applied to genres commonly regarded as the “mirror of reality” and the “voice of truth”—photojournalism and documentary photography.

Against this objective backdrop, you will readily notice in such photographs the manipulation of viewers through compositional techniques, the reshaping of narrative through framing, and the author's perspective on the subject or event—deliberately constructing what the viewer will accept as “witnessed with their own eyes.”

Alas, truth and truthfulness are not synonymous.

And now, having understood what the maximally realistic angle of our triangle looks like, let us turn to its other extreme condition.

In it, the photographer will fully discover their own voice, and we shall see where this leads.
