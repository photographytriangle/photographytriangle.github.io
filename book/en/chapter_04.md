Chapter 4: Subjective Photography

Chapter 4

Subjective Photography

In the second of the triangle's extreme conditions, among the three elements of photographic interaction, the role of the subject is maximally weakened. It does not disappear entirely—otherwise such a system could no longer be called photography—but becomes merely one of its auxiliary components. Its role may be even less significant than that of the light source employed during the shoot or the chosen focal length of the lens.

In this book, we will conventionally call such an extreme condition subjective photography.

If I had to choose a color to represent it, it would be red—as a symbol of active authorial position.

Subjective photography is an equally theoretical and unattainable point in reality—a vertex of our model—just as objective photography is.

The photographer in it is an embodiment of pure reason, consciously creating and seeking contact with the viewer through photographic means of expression. In this extreme condition, it would be more accurate to call him an author, but to maintain terminological consistency, we will continue to call him a photographer.

At this extreme point of the triangle, the photographer operates exclusively from logic and knows precisely what he wishes to communicate through his photograph. There is no room for either the unconscious or the incomprehensible. This is precisely why it is as inaccessible in reality as objective photography is.

The worldview from the subjective vertex of the triangle is, first and foremost, a perspective rooted in Western philosophical and scientific thought, in which defining the boundaries of consciousness and the possibility of communicating experience remain among the most crucial concerns across centuries.

Since the time of René Descartes, European philosophers have contemplated the isolation of consciousness and the possibility of communication only through the senses.

Modern science concurs. Neuropsychology confirms that each brain is isolated within its own boundaries and receives information about the world only through sensory channels. All our knowledge is formed from electrical signals originating from the sense organs.

Alas, the photographer has no way to directly convey his intention to the viewer.

True, neurointerface technologies enabling information transfer from one brain to another via specialized equipment have been under active development recently. Claims of up to 90% transmission success and the creation of “brain networks” for collaborative problem-solving have been made.

Yet if you examine the details, you find that these primarily involve the transmission of rudimentary binary messages (yes/no, start/stop) or the transfer of basic sensory perceptions and motor commands (up/down, hand movement). Attempts are also being made to recognize and transmit fundamental emotions (fear, joy, anger, surprise, interest, anxiety). But these are transmitted merely as a kind of “emotional vector” sensation capable of inducing a similar state in the recipient.

As you can understand, this very limited toolkit cannot convey an idea, a concept, or the feeling of love. Even if you receive a signal through a neurointerface and experience a sense of fear—it will be your fear. You know no more about how it feels to another person than you would from reading the word “fear” or looking at someone else's frightened face.

These technologies are powerless to overcome the distance between consciousnesses and can only narrow it.

The primary goal of subjective photography is to attempt to bridge this gap between photographer and viewer using photography's expressive language.

Yet the viewer need not be someone else. This “other” can be the photographer himself at the moment of viewing his own photograph. By first focusing and articulating his thoughts and experiences in the form of a photograph, then deciphering them as a message from his “past self,” the photographer can use subjective photography as an effective tool for shaping a “new self.” In this case, the goal of bridging the gap between consciousnesses does not disappear but transforms into inner work toward this “future self.”

To bridge this gap between photographer and viewer, one of the most crucial questions in subjective photography becomes the choice of language for transmitting the photographic message. And if you say that this language will be “the language of photography,” I would ask you to speak something in Cyrillic or Latin script.

A lengthy catalog of dozens of elements—encompassing the choice of subject, technical and geometric considerations, work with light and color, compositional techniques, and much else—bears greater resemblance to an alphabet than to a language.

Just as letters from a single alphabet can be employed across numerous natural languages, so too can a unified photographic alphabet enable the creation of countless photographic languages composed of them.

Like natural languages, photographic ones can be more or less widespread, rare, or dead.

Yet in order to select or develop a new language for transmitting one's photographic message, the photographer must first understand to which viewers he is addressing it, and which languages they are capable of comprehending.

For instance, a photographer may set as his goal to make his photographic language comprehensible to the majority of viewers. This desire to be understood by all will necessitate either the choice of the most widespread language or the narrowing of its vocabulary to elements uniformly perceived by the majority of people.

One such example of a most widespread photographic language can be seen in the style of images automatically applied when photographing with a modern smartphone. In such images you will find a combination of expanded tonal range, slightly elevated contrast, saturation, and sharpness. Smoothed pink skin and blurred backgrounds in portraits. Intensified colors of sky and foliage in landscapes. I imagine you understand what I am describing, as this visual style—combined with two or three basic compositional techniques and an overall “positive” tenor—constantly confronts you in media, social networks, and advertising.

You may or may not like such images, but this photographic language is the most widespread in the contemporary world. Though it is neither the only one nor native to viewers, the majority of them will nevertheless be able to perceive and comprehend the message conveyed through it. And if you wish to be understood by the majority, you should speak a language they can comprehend.

There is another way to make your image comprehensible to the maximum number of viewers. For this, you must develop your photographic language based on compositional techniques that are perceived at a biological level by all people, regardless of their culture or education.

For several decades now, psychologists and neurophysiologists have been conducting active research into the mechanisms of physiological perception. Some studies have included not only humans but other primates as well, demonstrating the evolutionary roots of at least part of what we consider beautiful or harmonious.

Here are some such biologically perceived elements of an image:

Associations of warm and cold colors with temperature: Research demonstrates that the connection between temperature and “warm” and “cold” colors is encoded already at the retinal level, which directly influences our physiological responses. We physically perceive red as warmer and blue as colder.

For example, when assessing room temperature, participants perceived it several degrees higher in a room painted red than when assessing temperature in a room with blue walls.

Left-side preference: Experiments involving humans, primates, birds, and mice demonstrate that the left visual field and the corresponding left side of the frame receive greater attention when perceiving an image. This left-side preference in image perception is based on the leading role of the right hemisphere in processing what we see.

For people who read left-to-right, this effect is additionally reinforced by learning and habit, yet even those who read right-to-left predominantly begin perceiving images from the left side.

A composition in which eye movement is intended to proceed from left to right will be more natural for the majority of viewers, regardless of their training or cultural specifics.

Physiology of atmospheric perspective: Two effects exerted the most profound influence on the evolution of our ancestors—longitudinal chromatic aberration in the eye and evolutionary adaptation to atmospheric light scattering.

The first consequence of this is that blue objects are perceived as more distant, while red ones are perceived as closer. This effect persists even when perceiving a single color against a neutral background: for example, orange on a white background appears nearer.

A second consequence emerges in the perception of saturation as information about scene depth. At equal brightness, more saturated colors feel closer, while less saturated colors appear more distant.

The maximum effect of “saturation-depth” is perceived in gradations of red against a neutral background.

A third consequence is that the viewer perceives high-contrast objects as positioned closer, while low-contrast objects appear more distant from them.

Thus, atmospheric perspective is not merely a compositional technique, but rather a reflection of the biological and evolutionary roots of our perception.

Brightness-based depth perception: In the absence of linear or atmospheric perspective, the brain switches to local brightness differences. Within a single object, darker regions will be perceived as more distant, while lighter regions will be perceived as protruding forward. Accordingly, on a monochromatic background, the viewer will consider a brighter object closer or higher than the others.

Biologically justified contour accentuation: When perceiving complex images, our eye responds unequally to different levels of detail. We are particularly attuned to contrasts in mid-sized details—not very large blobs or very minute specifics, but something intermediate, such as faces, figures, or objects. The stronger the contrast of these mid-range contours, the more noticeably they will “stand out” forward and appear closer.

You can use this effect both to accentuate the object you need in the frame and to reduce contour contrast of distracting objects.

This will operate at the biological level and redirect attention to the parts of the frame you need.

Pattern pleasingness: Research involving humans and primates has demonstrated that primary and secondary visual cortices contain neurons tuned to rapid detection and “reassurance” upon encountering repeated elements. Repetition signals safety, stability, and the absence of hidden threats. In photography, one can use the repetition of identical elements to organize the frame and calm the viewer. Conversely, one can disrupt rhythm to attract attention to that part of the image.

Attractiveness of symmetry: Not only humans, but other primates as well, prefer more symmetrical faces to less symmetrical ones. This is confirmed by activation of neurons in the visual cortex, which points to the evolutionary causes of this.

However, one should not attempt to make a model's face absolutely symmetrical: research shows that ideal symmetry is not perceived as an ideal of beauty. On the contrary, a touch of natural asymmetry makes the appearance more attractive than absolute symmetry.

Leading lines: Experiments reveal that neurons responding to light become activated when leading lines in a static image create a sense of motion. People from different cultures, without any training in art or photography, and even preschool children automatically follow their gaze along leading lines in the frame.

Non-biological nature of the golden ratio: Research on the perception of facial harmony has shown that optimal proportions preferred by viewers are 36% for the distance from eyes to mouth relative to face length, and 46% for interpupillary distance relative to face width. That is, faces perceived as beautiful turn out to be narrower than the golden ratio requires.

Another study confirmed that fewer than half of participants perceive golden ratio proportions as most attractive. The golden ratio as an “ideal of beauty” does not arise from the biological mechanisms of our perception. And if you harmonize facial proportions during retouching, consider instead these experimentally determined ratios.

This is far from an exhaustive list, but bear in mind that many contemporary studies are conducted on relatively small samples. However, results are constantly being refined, and increasingly reliable and accurate data about the biological nature of these and other compositional techniques continue to emerge.

By grounding your photographic language in them, you will render your images comprehensible to viewers regardless of their age, region, culture, or education.

Regional differences in image perception also exist, comparable to widespread national languages.

And while serious and consistent research on differences between Northern and Southern perception is extremely scarce, differences between Eastern and Western perception are well-studied and quite substantial.

Differences in social organization, artistic traditions, and architecture result in distinctive perceptual characteristics among people of Asian and European cultures. Research identifies several key differences:

Western viewers expect contrastive emphasis of the central element and prefer minimalist backgrounds. Asian viewers, by contrast, devote greater attention to overall frame harmony, value detailed, multi-layered backgrounds, and perceive the general interrelationships between elements. They are more responsive to symmetry and balance among image components.

These differences are reinforced by research on visual organization: Europeans tend to seek clear structure and simplicity in images, whereas Chinese participants are more tolerant of visual diversity, perceiving complexity as part of aesthetics.

However, cultural boundaries do not align with state borders. For instance, in an international study of perception of complex scenes, substantial differences were identified between individuals from Asian and Western countries. Yet participants from Eastern and Western Turkey, influenced by different cultural spheres, demonstrated significant divergence in perception.

Yet the most striking differences we see emerge in differences in the meanings of colors in Asian and Western countries.

In European culture, white is traditionally associated with purity and is used as the color of wedding dress, whereas in Asia, by contrast, it is the color of mourning, worn at funerals. Yellow in Asia symbolizes imperial authority and sanctity, while in Europe it signifies jealousy or betrayal. Red in Asia represents luck, joy, and prosperity. For Europeans, however, it evokes associations not only with danger but also with passion.

However, in the contemporary world, many rituals and practices often intersect with one another. One can readily encounter Asian brides dressed in white—due to globalization, wedding fashion is beginning to blend cultural traditions. One or another representative of Western or Eastern culture may adhere to the norms of the opposite region as a result of education, personal preference, or professional activity.

Despite the traditional top-to-bottom reading direction in China, horizontal reading among regional representatives proved faster and yielded better retention. Contemporary Asian viewers have adapted to horizontal reading through internet use, education, and work.

The conclusion you can draw from this when formulating your image's “photographic language” is: avoid the naive categorization of people into “Asians” and “Europeans.” Regional particularities of perception are an objective fact, and they will influence people from the corresponding region to varying degrees. But beyond this, there are also other factors that will exert no less influence.

Just as you will easily identify in “photographic language” analogs of regional languages or dialects. In various religious, professional, or social communities you will find their own “visual language”—gestures, angles, color combinations, iconic objects comprehensible only to the in-group.

Enumerating these visual codes for each such group would be counterproductive and would divert this book from its primary focus. However, let me note—as someone who has photographed subcultures—that this “visual language” is quite subtle. The smaller such a group, the more critical it becomes for its members that you understand and deploy this language precisely. How much you, as a photographer, deserve their trust.

When formulating your message for a closed community, you must exercise considerably greater care in the choice of compositional techniques than when shooting for generic “Europeans” or “men”.

A “photographic language” comprehensible to only a single specific person is also possible. Constructed from signs known only to the two of you, it will resemble a secret message between intimates.

Yet deliberate “nonsense,” constructed as a thoughtful edifice, can convey meaning to the viewer, becoming the photographic analog of Oberiu poetry or Lewis Carroll's verse language.

Many techniques employed by absurdists can be transposed onto the language of photography. Logical rupture might be conveyed through sharp deviation from the rule of thirds and compositional voids. Puns might be achieved through double exposure and layering of objects. Repetitions and variations—through discordant rhythms of patterns. Paradoxes—through clashing colors. The transition from reality to the grotesque—through the mixture of documentary and advertising styles. An open ending—through unexpected framing that does not tell a complete story.

Moreover, it is precisely in subjective photography, grounded in logic and reason, that this acquires special significance. If your goal is to disrupt the viewer's automatic perception rooted in familiar templates, to compel them to regard the structure of order from an unfamiliar angle, then “visual absurdism” can become a powerful and effective language.

Another conclusion you can draw from the similarity between photographic language and natural languages is an understanding of the finiteness of the “photographic sentence.”

Photography, like a sentence, has a beginning and an end. It is impossible to convey an infinite internal monologue through the means of the frame. This finiteness of the photographic phrase inevitably leads to incompleteness in the meanings transmitted, just as the photograph's frame limits itself to a single chosen fragment of reality. Since the goal of subjective photography is to convey thought to the viewer as precisely as possible, this requires a strict limitation of the “length” of visual utterance.

All of the above leads us to the conclusion that even in an ideal situation, mutual understanding between photographer and viewer will be possible only when all the aforementioned factors converge.

The photographer's “circle of expression” is not ideal. The message they wish to convey to the viewer will be incomplete due to the limitation of its length and inevitable inaccuracies in translation to the chosen photographic language.

The viewer's “circle of perception” is similarly imperfect. It resides at the intersection of the biological, cultural, and social languages they know, are able to understand, or can perceive.

Only in the zone of intersection between the photographer's circle of expression and the viewer's circle of perception can successful communication between them occur.

Nevertheless, in any photograph there will always be present the phenomenon of “hidden meanings.”

Since the viewer is a full co-author of the image, the perception of compositional elements and framing by a viewer with a different “circle of perception” can lead to their decoding in a different manner, or even in a diametrically opposite way.

For instance, photographs created with the aim of glorifying a particular idea are often perceived by its opponents with precise opposition. And images created in the language of a small community, containing for its participants a profound meaning, may appear absurd or aesthetically unappealing to those outside it.

The miracle of photography lies precisely in this: it is a bottomless “box of meanings,” containing far more than the photographer intended or any particular viewer is capable of perceiving.

“Hidden meanings” are inevitable, but if the photographer has failed to formulate their message clearly—then these hidden meanings become the primary content of their image.

As for the technical aspects of subjective photography, note that the sole criterion of “correctness” or “incorrectness” of image parameters lies in how effectively they communicate the intention to the chosen viewer. Whether they correspond to the realistic criteria of objective photography is entirely irrelevant.

What would be considered distortions in objective photography becomes a working tool in subjective photography and will be welcomed precisely if it brings the image closer to the photographer's intention.

The defining characteristic of subjective photography is not its departure from realism or how “creative” a message it conveys. Yes, the photographer can use it to communicate a concept, tell a story, or express their position. But it can equally be their questions to themselves, their memories, or their internal monologue.

The essential feature of subjective photography is the voice of the photographer, which resonates loudest here. And it resounds precisely in the language of photography itself.

Have you not noticed at contemporary photography exhibitions that you often spend more time reading than visually perceiving images? Photographs that are hardly expressive, accompanied by five pages of conceptual description, appear quite frequently there.

The language of these works communicates its message to a select few connoisseurs. Yet the rarefied nature of this language contradicts the commercial imperative to present these images to viewers, the majority of whom do not command such a language. They require textual guidance—explaining what one should think about these images, lest they accidentally mistake them for what they might appear to be from the viewers' “circle of perception.”

And in each such case, one is left only to speculate: did the photographer fail to convey their thought through photographic means alone, or are you simply not their audience—and have no place in that gallery.

Photography, as a visual art form, possesses an extensive arsenal of expressive techniques and is fully capable of communicating with viewers in its own language rather than in the language of literature, video, or augmented reality. If we do venture beyond its boundaries—this is entirely legitimate, but we must be conscious of this choice and understand precisely why we are making it.

And now, having thoroughly explored what the subjective angle of our triangle represents, let us journey toward its third “extreme condition.”

And the closer we approach it, the quieter the author's insistent voice will become.
