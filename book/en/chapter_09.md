Chapter 9: The New Reality

Chapter 9

The New Reality

You cannot ignore this, even if you wanted to. The emergence and rapid evolution of neural networks have already affected the lives of hundreds of millions of people, one way or another, and this is only the beginning.

The images they generate pose an acute question before us—what is photography, really? Where are its boundaries? Should we reinforce them or completely reconsider them?

Today, AI-generated images are visually indistinguishable from photographs for most viewers, and even specialists need time to determine whether they're looking at a real photo or a generated image. They don't simply compete with photography—they are actively displacing it thanks to instant creation, low cost, and the absence of skill requirements beyond formulating a text prompt.

This book is devoted to photography, the interactions that create it, and a tool for analyzing them. But these interactions, as you may have already noticed, are universal and, like infinite axes, extend far beyond photography as a form of visual art.

Let's make use of this and examine AI-generated images from each vertex of the Photography Triangle.

First, let's recall what lies behind objective photography and what constitutes its very essence.

Of the three elements of photographic interaction, the photographer and camera are maximally weakened in it, which brings the viewer's perception of the subject to the foreground.

What will we see when looking at AI-generated images from the perspective of objective photography?

The place of the photographer has been taken by the prompter—a person formulating text requests, and the place of the camera has been taken by the generative model being used.

Both the photographer and the prompter create images, but what is the difference between them?

To describe the work of a photographer, the Latin word artifex would be most accurate—a master and artist who creates art through their mind and hands and possesses technical skill.

And to describe what the prompter does, the Latin word consultans—one who seeks counsel from an oracle for a “vision”—would be far more fitting. And if the supplication to the neuro-oracle is formulated correctly, they will receive that “vision” in the form of a generated image.

This is a very revealing detail, but one that barely influences anything within objective photography. Don't forget that the prompter's influence, just like the photographer's influence in objective photography, is minimal.

The viewer's role has not changed at all, and they may not even suspect the most crucial detail. The objects they perceive in the AI-generated image, formed by the combination of brightness, contrast, and compositional elements—do not exist in reality.

Photography served as an imperfect yet necessary vessel, conveying to the viewer the precious contents of reality.

AI-generated images, by contrast, are an empty vessel, delivering not a single drop of it to them.

From the vertex of objective photography, you can clearly see legalized piracy in image‑generation platforms. The datasets used to train neural networks were unlawfully obtained by copying images from all available sources without the permission of the rights holders. Numerous lawsuits filed against developer companies by classical media outlets, photo banks, and private photographers are typically resolved through settlement or dismissed by courts based on legal technicalities. Not a single developer company has attempted to contest the accusations on their merits, building no defense based on evidence of the legality of the datasets they used to build their business.

Generations of photographers, curators, and specialists created world photography as a form of visual art. Generations of viewers evaluated it, participating in the photographic process and selecting the best works.

Photographers or media outlets whose images were used without their knowledge can at least attempt to contest their rights in court. Millions and millions of viewers whose attention was stolen along with the images lack such an opportunity even theoretically. This very “concentrate of meaning,” formed by photography and viewer attention, is the true object of appropriation by developer companies. It is this concentrated human visual experience that has allowed generative models to develop so rapidly.

Modern AI images are essentially a cocktail mixed on the basis of real photographs. The next generations of image generators will inevitably, to a greater degree, be trained on already-generated content due to its visual indistinguishability from photographs.

The consequence of this is the inevitable degradation of that universal human visual experience, the appropriation and use of which allows AI giants to reap their fleeting profit today.

No technological innovation in history has been so destructive to human visual culture. All new technologies in the past allowed for the creation of images faster, but that speed was limited by the simple fact that humans applied them. Today, endless streams of visual spam are generated by bots, not people. When painting was displaced by photography, the speed of image production increased from pedestrian pace to automobile speed. The speed at which bot factories generate images today is the speed of a jet missile.

From the perspective of objective photography, an image is a two-dimensional representation of an object or scene that existed in physical reality.

One generative image, almost indistinguishable to the viewer from a photograph—is a lie. Their avalanche-like multiplying collective, before our very eyes replacing the captured reality—is cancer, already afflicting visual culture.

What this will ultimately lead to, we will be able to see for ourselves very soon.

This perspective is bound to give one pause. But in order to try to balance it, let's attempt to view what is happening from the perspective of subjective photography. Perhaps from there, the situation will look less grim?

When viewed from the subjective vertex, we first and foremost see the prompter, into which the author has transformed. His goal is the same—to transcend the boundaries of his consciousness and find contact with the viewer through the images he creates.

He still represents an “I,” conscious of the world and itself through its inner monologue. The judgments, evaluations, and self-descriptions of this “I” can be accomplished only through one of the natural languages in which this inner monologue takes place.

It is precisely in natural language that the prompter formulates his request for image generation. His thought will nonetheless be transformed into combinations of brightness, contrast, and compositional elements, but now not through the photographic process, but through the generative model.

As for the complete absence of a subject to photograph—from the subjective vertex, it was always merely one of the means of expressing the author's creative intent. The absence of this physically existing object allows for an additional degree of freedom—no longer is it necessary to search for a model or subject, to select costumes or wait for the right weather, to adjust lighting or choose a camera angle.

But if you think that from the perspective of subjective photography, the liberation from a subject to photograph or the use of natural language makes generative images attractive—then you would be greatly mistaken.

Subjective photography aims at forming solidarity between author and viewers. This exchange of images helps separate “I”s, through the commonality of experience, to feel themselves as a single “we.” It is precisely this humanistic goal that makes the visual language itself—the very language in which such communication becomes possible—one of the principal values from the perspective of subjective photography.

Any actions leading to the degradation of visual language can be perceived from the subjective vertex of the triangle exclusively as a step toward the destruction of human solidarity and visual segregation.

The visual language of an image is not the individual compositional elements; it is the meaning they acquire in a particular cultural context. And it is precisely these culturally conditioned meanings that serve as parts of speech from which the author can construct a phrase of his visual message.

When compiling the datasets on which generative networks were trained, both the cultural context and the meanings of various elements within it were nearly completely lost. Yes, thanks to vast arrays of images, various combinations of colors, objects, or styles will purely statistically coincide with the theme of the text prompt. But this is an extremely vulgarized and generalized vocabulary of the language you need.

The very visual message itself, which uses these elements as parts of speech, is neither analyzed by the neural network during training nor created by it during generation.

A generative image is a collection of words related to a particular theme and not forming any meaningful message.

You can easily verify this with the example of natural, not visual, language. Try asking any neural network to generate a large image of a hand pointing to a line of clearly readable text in a book. Specify the historical and cultural context that makes sense to you, but give no instructions regarding the text itself. Look at the mangled words mixed with illegible symbols and containing grammatical errors. Even if individual words can be read and even somehow relate to the specified period—their totality will carry no meaning whatsoever.

The same thing happens with visual language. Caught in the meat grinder of generative intelligence, fragments of other people's photographs are squeezed out to you in the form of multicolored, but quite homogeneous mincemeat.

Any person, even imitating other people's creativity and not understanding the details of what is being copied, will nonetheless convey some part of it, even if distorted. Moreover, what is being copied will inevitably pass through the prism of their personal cultural context, enriching the copy with new layers of meaning. Generative networks, on the other hand, do not copy; they imitate the presence of meaning.

Yes, an author can, through repeated refinements and the composition of a detailed prompt, attempt to bring the generation result under his control. Moreover, we could even reason that the composition of a generation request can be compared to the creation of a graphic language of an image and will contain both its own “compositional techniques” and the creation of the necessary “composition of the request” from them.

But such an authorial approach to image generation will in no way be able to influence what is happening as a whole.

The vast majority of generative content is created not by people, but by bots, and the request for generation to the graphic imitator is composed by a linguistic imitator. The external similarity of these images to photographs and the avalanche-like speed of their spread already now lead to the inevitable—the degradation of visual languages.

Chaotically mixed elements, no longer carrying meaning, gradually train the viewer to abandon the perception of anything in images except their external form.

The developers of generative models are private companies, whose primary goal is to generate income for their shareholders. Everything they do will be subordinate to this goal.

In order to increase profits, they need to make their imperfect products accepted and used by the majority of users, which can be achieved in various ways.

To implement the analysis of photographic message in dataset images, as well as to account for it when generating images from a request, is hardly a technically feasible task. And even if it were possible, it would require giant investments in development that would not allow for quick profits.

But there is another way to make the already existing developments more acceptable to the majority—not to prevent their application, which leads to the degradation of overall visual culture. Against such a background, their developments will look quite acceptable and be applied everywhere.

This path requires no investment, is legally safe, and given the current speed of “noise‑flooding” of visual culture with spam content—is quite rapid.

There is no malice in this, just that business efficiency is always the ratio of invested costs to achieved results.

But from the perspective of humanity as a whole, both the actions and inactions of companies aimed at destroying visual language lead to one thing—the deliberate division of mankind.

This is a moral crime and a violation of the foundations of ethics. This is an attempt to destroy universal human solidarity. This is a communicative tragedy leading to the loss of mutual understanding.

And this is that rare case when the voices speaking from both the objective and subjective peaks of the triangle converge in their conclusions.

These voices are heard perfectly well down there, in the middle-earth. But the only thing they evoke here is a slight condescending smile, the kind adults smile at children discussing Santa Claus.

It is here that both those who created these developments and those who use them are found.

Here it is no secret to anyone that at the foundation of this technology lies images obtained dishonestly. But everyone uses them, knowing that the police will not come for you personally.

Yes, the owner of a bot factory generating streams of visual spam is, at an accelerated pace, helping to build a fragmented world that has lost part of its culture and its sense of reality—a world in which his children will have to live tomorrow. But today he will feed them ice cream and take them to the water park.

Probably, an editor, instead of purchasing an image, generating a colored “plug” for an advertising message, is not allowing photography to develop, and this will lead to its rapid stagnation. But he did it instantly, and the “lifespan” of this message will not exceed a couple of days anyway.

Yes, most likely the image of a generative artist is meaningless, but who and how will prove it? After all, some meaning will be perceived even in a random set of words. Perhaps that is what he meant…

Middle-earth is enormous. This is not a place where principles matter. Here everyone understands everything. This is precisely what allows image generation technology to develop successfully and at an accelerated pace and quickly displace everything else.

This technology — undoubtedly has a successful and promising future.

But if we begin moving toward the perceptual peak, all this noise of the battle between idealism and pragmatism begins to subside, gradually turning into silence.

If we are all already in the turbulent stream, what is the point of shouting at it or pounding the water with our fists?

Yes, it is easiest to relax and splash around merrily in it, paying no attention to the approaching roar of the waterfall. Yes, one can try rowing against the current for as long as strength lasts, hoping to return back to the source.

But instead, one can try to swim across to the other shore and continue the journey, leaving the stream behind.

And to find the right direction to that shore, let us look at generative images from the perceptual peak of the triangle.

The external viewer in perceptual practice has no significance, and the true viewer becomes not the photographer, but the prompter using a generative model to create images. But how should we relate to the subject of the shot that is explicitly absent?

Will this absence be a reason for you to completely refuse working with generative images?

This will depend primarily on what you personally have located beyond the perceptual peak.

If you are a nonbeliever—you will most likely consider any image, regardless of its origin, merely a stimulus provoking a reaction in your psyche. Does it really matter to you whether this stimulus was captured by a camera or created by an algorithm?

If you are a believer—will you consider the place of the absent object filled with divine presence? Or conversely, will you perceive it as a violation of the order of things and a perversion of the supreme design?

If you are a Buddhist—then since the essence of any form is emptiness, the absence of this form will not have substantial significance.

I will suggest that for the majority of those who practice the perceptual path, the very absence of the photographic object will not in itself be a reason to refuse using generative images.

But unfortunately, AI-generated images have other characteristics that complicate their use in perceptual work. Let us mark out the most fundamental of them.

First, generated images are statistical patterns obtained through the analysis of arrays of photographs and modulated by your request. One could call the object in generative images the visual patterns extracted from the works of other authors and combined into a given form.

All those photographs that underlie your generative image have already passed through the perception process of the photographers who captured them.

Look at such an image as a stained-glass window made up of small shards of different glass. Some of them are transparent, some almost black, some colored, and almost all of them are somehow distorted. Yes, some light can pass through such a window, but seeing what lies behind it will be practically impossible.

Second, the generative model forms an image based precisely on a text prompt, which is incompatible with the cessation of internal monologue. Any changes that will need to be made will also require you to verbalize them.

How, in this case, should you technically organize active observation? Entering this state is not easy, and to use a generative model you must enter and exit it repeatedly and almost instantaneously?

Third, most generative models are trained in advance and do not make changes to their work as a result of executing requests. They do not learn anything new, there is no accumulation of meaning in the process of interaction with users, they cannot have insights or revelations.

The results you get are like an endless rotation of a kaleidoscope. Each time the result will differ somewhat from the previous one, but completely new colors or forms will never appear.

Fourth, generative models do not even provide the randomness of a kaleidoscope.

To generate an image, they select the pattern that best matches the text of your request among all photographs on which they were trained. What you get without clarifying your request is an averaged image that resembles the most banal of already existing shots on the topic of your request.

If you ask to create “something more original,” they simply select a rarer pattern from those already available. As a result, you get a slightly less banal image, but still similar to what has already been shot.

Even the listed reasons may be sufficient to completely refuse using generative models and once again take a camera in your hands.

Unfortunately, the current implementation of this technology is designed not for you, but for the quick satisfaction of requests from an average and not very demanding user.

But, strangely enough, it is precisely this thought that compels one not to abandon attempts and to continue searching for ways to use AI images in perceptual practice.

The fact is that the same can be said about the camera itself.

Decades of camera development were aimed at achieving the same goal—obtaining a “correct” picture without the need to understand photographic principles. The democratization of photography in the early 20th century took place under Kodak's slogan “You press the button, we do the rest.” The gradually appearing automatic exposure, automatic aperture, TTL light meter, program mode, autofocus in cameras served precisely this goal.

The sum of these developments led to the emergence and mass distribution of the “point and shoot” concept. The emergence of digital cameras led to the possibility of instantly obtaining an image. Computational photography in modern smartphones led to the automation of even image processing, as well as the elimination of the need to “capture the moment” through pre-capture technology. The emergence of generative models is merely another logical step in the same direction, finally allowing one to abandon the camera itself.

With each successive step of automation, users willingly exchanged part of their creative freedom for the convenience of using the technology. But the price paid for this convenience was a gradual narrowing of the ability to influence the resulting image with each new automatic function.

The current implementation of generative models completely deprives the user of the ability to make changes to the resulting image within the model. He can either accept the generation result as it is, or repeat it anew. Complete convenience naturally demands complete abandonment of creative freedom.

But not everyone was willing to give up this freedom. In parallel with the automation of photography, photographers engaged in creative photography also gradually learned to bypass the camera's intrusive assistance or use its algorithms unconventionally to achieve their desired results.

Depending on the goals before them, this could be either a complete rejection of automatic functions, or the use of randomness to destroy predictability in lomography, or the use of non-standard optics or pinhole cameras, or shooting with long exposures, or creating their own cameras or complete rejection of cameras when creating photograms.

All of these, as well as many other ways to influence the photographic process or camera operation, served one common goal—to regain control over image creation and to decide oneself how it should look and what parameters it should correspond to.

But perceptual practice allows one to look at the problem more broadly.

Seeing in a photographer with a camera or prompter with a generative model an inseparable pair, it does not blame specific technologies for everything, but points to the prerequisites for creating stereotypical images according to established patterns in the consciousness of the photographer or prompter.

Regaining control over image creation from a perceptual perspective is necessary not as an end in itself, but as a means to overcome stereotypical perception of reality, which in most cases differs little from the fully automatic mode of camera operation.

And if the ways to circumvent camera algorithms in perceptual practice have already been developed, then the obstacles to using generative models in it seem insurmountable at the moment.

Nevertheless, I have not abandoned hope that a way to overcome these obstacles might exist.

I want to believe that in time we will be able to find the vulnerable points, “cracks in the program,” as Vilém Flusser described them, through which we can break through into the space of freedom and make generative models, if not our partners in perceptual practice, then at least its convenient tools.

If we continue these attempts, then sooner or later a solution will be found.

But for now—use a camera to achieve your goals. It has been and remains both a faithful instrument in the hands of a photographer capturing reality, and a megaphone amplifying the voice of the author, and a reliable support for one walking the difficult path of inner transformation.
