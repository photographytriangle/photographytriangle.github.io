What This Book Is About and How It Can Help You

What This Book Is About and How It Can Help You

My name is Alexander Zabara. I’m a photographer, color grader, and educator. I’ve been working with retouching and color correction for almost 30 years, and I’ve been teaching image processing for about 15 years.

In developing my image processing course, I was faced with an essential need: to create a universal framework—a model to guide the processing of any photograph.

My challenge was to teach photographers of vastly different backgrounds and styles. The framework I needed had to be genuinely universal—independent of genre, of the photographer’s personality, and of their worldview. I needed a system that encompassed all of photography: one that honored both its technical demands and its creative potential.

Hoping to find the answer in established research, I turned to the classical texts on photographic theory.

But, to my great surprise, I discovered that no such unified system existed.

None of the systems created by photographic theorists were universal. The authors of these works focused on specific technical, genre-based, or ideological aspects, and their theories didn’t encompass photography as a whole.

The authors of books on the philosophy of photography, for the most part, were literary scholars or critics, not photographers. They saw photography primarily as a cultural phenomenon, and their theories were not intended for practical application by photographers.

And none of these theories could be directly applied to the process of color correction.

Based on this research, I began to independently search for patterns in photography that could serve as the foundation for such a universal system of description. The imperfection of existing theories, despite their variety, suggested that choosing any particular basic elements of photography as the foundation would not allow for describing all the diversity that photography can represent.

This led me to the idea that the foundation of such a universal system should not be individual basic elements of photography, but rather the process of interaction between them.

Both individual elements of photographic interaction and some of the connections between them had already been found by different authors. But the unified system they form, and the common logic of these connections, apparently had not been previously recognized.

Built on the basis of these interactions, the system I named the Photography Triangle, even in its basic form, worked quite well in accomplishing its task. It served as a guide, helping photographers understand the general direction of image processing and determine the order and necessity of various operations.

But as I continued analyzing the interactions underlying this system, I gradually began to realize that I had stumbled upon something far more significant than simply a “color correction assistant.”

The Photography Triangle allowed me to coherently place within itself compositional techniques, elements of the creative process, ideas and philosophical concepts. It could be applied not only for analyzing photographs, but beyond their bounds.

At its core lies a very simple triangular system, the work with which most resembles using a compass.

A compass cannot find your global coordinates or give you a detailed understanding of the terrain. But it will help you get oriented and on this basis independently seek the path you need.

The Photography Triangle is capable of becoming for you an analog of a compass, helping you navigate the world of photography, while not giving, by itself, answers to all questions. It was neither invented by me nor taken as a given.

On the pages of this book, I invite you to join me, step by step, to find its basic elements, the ways they interact, and to draw the remarkable conclusions to which they will lead us in the end.

But there is also another, personal reason that prompted me to write this book.

Photography gave me a language with which I could not only convey my feelings to others, but also better understand them myself. But the more deeply I immersed myself in the study of composition, color correction, and shooting rules, the more I began to lose interest in photography itself. Instead of the “magic of the moment,” I began to see in photography only a lifeless construction of pixels.

And then I tried to apply the Photography Triangle for something it was never originally intended for—to analyze my own position in the field of photography and to assess where I wanted to go.

Through this, I was able to reinvent myself as a photographer, find new meanings, and understand where to go next.

Perhaps this book will help some of you look at your shooting and color correction process in a new light. And if it inspires even one person to pick up their camera again, I will consider it a success.

Chapter 1: Coordinates Verification

Chapter 1

Coordinates Verification

Before moving to the main part of the book, I believe it necessary to make this small digression.

Photography as a phenomenon is too vast and varied for anyone to describe it with absolute certainty. The very word, derived from the Greek φωτός, “light,” and γράφω, “write,” has three meanings that layer upon each other.

On one hand, it is the very process of recording light and obtaining an image. On the other—the result of that process, in the form of a print or digital photograph. But it is also a form of visual art, possessing its own visual language and expressive means.

A word with such enormous diversity of meanings simply cannot precisely delineate the boundaries of a phenomenon without necessary additional clarification. It can only outline an approximate zone in which the object of our interest lies, like a watercolor sketch on wet paper.

Attempts to speak seriously about the essence of photography almost always reduce to quasi-religious convictions about the nature of truth or falsehood, the validity of which the speaker is prepared to defend with appropriate fervor. And some of the topics touching on photography are a kind of “litmus test,” helping us better understand our interlocutor’s worldview.

I am aware that my perspective on photography is not objective. Like each of us—in it there are many personal opinions and preferences, some of which I can prove, others which I only sense as most truthful.

We have discussed such complex questions about photography many times with colleagues and students, and with many of them we remain friends to this day, despite opposite points of view. But since direct dialogue between us in a book is impossible, I find it necessary to clarify for you my perspective on some of these contentious topics. This will help you understand me better and “align our coordinates.”

“The Holiness of Film and the Sinfulness of Digital”

Fortunately, the heated debates about the superiority of “film” over “digital” have long since subsided.

In the early 2000s, film photographers did not love the extremely imperfect digital cameras of that time and lacked mastery of the nuances of processing their digital images.

I not only always agreed with their arguments about the shortcomings of digital photography, but also supplemented this list with a multitude of problems they were utterly unaware of.

We parted ways only in our conclusions—I do not believe that only analog photography is capable of conveying the elusive “magic of the moment.” For me, this is rather a shift from admiration for the very “magic of the moment” to veneration of one of the technologies by which it is captured.

Analog technology inspires not mere respect, but reverence. Photography as a visual art has been shaped, in part, by its limitations. To this day in the digital process, we continue to draw upon its achievements and sometimes imitate features that have long since transitioned from liabilities to style.

For me, film technology is like clear window glass, separating us from the stage of reality. Digital, on the other hand, is lower-grade glass, covered in dust and smudges. But if you learn to use a sponge and cleaning solution properly, after cleaning it, you can see through it almost as clearly.

If you have the desire and means, shoot on film, learning the development and printing process. If you use a digital camera, preserving the “magic of the moment” will require additional effort.

It is precisely on the basis of this attitude toward technology that I am not willing to unconditionally reject generative images solely on the grounds of their origin.

But we will examine them with you in detail in the final part of this book.

“Virgin Pixels”

Another common perspective is the notion of image “inviolability” and the destruction of some inherent “nature” within it during post-processing.

“I shot it this way, I didn’t paint it!” such a photographer declares proudly.

For me, this is merely a variation on the “truthfulness of film” applied to digital photography.

The argumentation put forward by supporters of this view is roughly as follows. Any processing changes the original pixels captured by the camera. The “authentic” pixels that were shot will be replaced by new, “synthetic” ones generated by the processing software. Therefore, processing destroys the “authenticity” of the captured image.

This idea does not hold up to scrutiny if one looks more closely at the camera sensor. Most digital cameras capture color images using one of the variants of the Bayer pattern. If you are not familiar with its structure, I recommend looking at its schematic representation in any accessible source.

Half of the cells on the sensor are covered with green color filters, a quarter with red, and a quarter with blue.

But to obtain three color channels from a single composite channel, the RAW converter must fill in the existing gaps using an averaging algorithm based on the pixels captured during the shooting.

It is precisely this computation, rather than capture, that obtained half the information in the green channel and three-quarters of the information in the red and blue channels of each image. If two-thirds of the “original” file were already computed, then what “truthfulness” or “inviolability” can we possibly speak of?

In response, you might hear: “That is exactly why I don’t shoot in RAW!”

But this simply means we are delegating such computation to the camera’s processor. The photograph will not become more “truthful” as a result. In the camera settings, you can see that sharpness, contrast, saturation, and much else for the future JPEG are all adjusted in any case. The only difference is that this adjustment must be made before shooting rather than after.

The computational algorithms carry no “truth” in the same way that adjustments will not impose the “curse” of your subjectivity upon the file.

Since the information in your file will be synthesized anyway, it makes sense to at least take this process under your own control.

“The Chasm Separating Photography and Drawing”

It might seem absurd to compare them. In photography, we record an image using photosensitive material or a digital sensor. In drawing, we use graphic tools.

I do not dispute this, but I want to draw your attention to the fact that once digitized, both a drawing and a photograph consist of pixels.

A pixel is merely a cell in a grid containing a numerical value in the color model being used. It has no built-in “history” or “memory.” There is no indication in it whether it was captured or drawn. What becomes paramount is not how the image pixels were obtained, but how it will be perceived by the viewer and what it will mean to them.

For example, a drawing made by a hyperrealist artist will be perceived by the viewer as a photograph, while an image created by a generative model from the prompt “watercolor portrait”—they will consider a drawing.

If you are searching for “authenticity,” it does not disappear upon digitization. The large-scale structures and characteristic features of painting, photography, and generative images are preserved even after digitization, and will not vanish even during subsequent color correction.

So when attempting to draw a boundary between drawing and photography, it is worth keeping two proverbs in mind: “First impressions are misleading” and “You never get a second chance to make a first impression.”

“The Chasm Not Separating Photography and Retouching”

Before me lie a graphics tablet, a stylus, and an airbrush pen. It is with these tools that I perform retouching, and these are drawing tools.

Does a retouched photograph remain a photograph, or does it become a drawing?

Typically, the answer to this question sounds like this. When retouching a photograph, we merely modify already captured information rather than create it “from scratch” on a blank canvas, as in a drawing.

Unfortunately, this view does not correspond to the reality of the contemporary processing workflow. In it, we not only clone already existing image fragments using a stamp tool. We also introduce new information into it, both through drawing tools and by inputting desired color values directly as numerical data.

One of the principal methods of skin retouching, based on frequency separation, is fundamentally not photographic in nature, and modern generative tools that create new image areas transform retouching into collage-making. Nevertheless, no one is in any hurry to acknowledge retouched images as drawings—this would be commercially disadvantageous.

A well-retouched image will not only continue to be viewed by the viewer as a photograph. Instead, it will be seen as more photographic than before retouching, even though almost no unaltered information will remain in it.

Since the viewer looking at an image has no direct way to distinguish what has been captured, retouched, or drawn, they will apply or not apply the label “photography” to the image based on their visual experience and individual understanding of reality.

Questions about the criteria for falsification and its impact on society are more pressing than ever right now. And laws being enacted in various countries requiring advertisers to indicate the fact of retouching, similar to tobacco and alcohol health warnings, demonstrate not a theoretical but a practical aspect of this issue.

And, to sum up all that has been said above, I would like to share with you a memory.

Because we all come from childhood.

The old shed was made of scraps. Crooked logs clad in rickety plywood sheets, shelves, a door, and not a single window.

It was filled with mysterious and dangerous things—rusty parts, old tools, samovars, and poisonous fertilizers.

Climbing into the shed was strictly forbidden.

It was into that shed I would slip whenever I got the chance, in order to quietly study the contents of its shelves.

But the most magical thing in the shed was the light. On a sunny day, a trembling image of the tree across from it would appear on the wall, inverted with its crown pointing down—a hole in the wall had created my very first camera obscura. And through the holes in the roof, several beams of sunlight pierced the shed clean through, like a magician’s swords through his box.

The illusion of reality of these light rods was so complete that climbing into the shed, I would each time try to grasp one or another of them, knowing full well that my fingers would pass through empty air again. But when I began to examine these rays close up—the illusion disappeared.

There was no real surface to the rays. Simply dust particles, stirred up by me in the old shed, would fly in and out of the narrow zone beneath the hole in the roof, for a second falling into a ray of bright light. They silently flew in a strange dance, changing with my every movement. But as soon as I stepped back slightly—the vision of a ray of light, which has surface and volume, returned again, despite knowing how it was constructed.

For me, photography is what shines through the pixels, beyond the Bayer pattern, beyond how its pixels came to acquire their values.

It is the light that reveals itself, falling upon the ever-changing dust of digital color channel values in the image.

If you search for the “truth of pixels,” you can end up with nothing but a handful of dust in a clenched fist.

It is better to search for truth in the light.

And do not be afraid to alter your photographs.

Chapter 2: Interacting Elements

Chapter 2

Interacting Elements

Each step of the image processing workflow requires you to make a small decision. Should you correct the white balance or not? Should you alter the brightness and contrast? Apply this to the entire photograph or only locally? Increase saturation or convert the image to black and white? Should you introduce additional colors into it?

And so on, and so forth…

And for these individual steps to form a chain leading in the right direction rather than turning in place or walking in circles, we need the equivalent of a compass to determine our course along this path.

A compass will not transport us magically to the final destination of our journey, but it will help us understand which direction to pursue steadfastly and long to reach our goal.

A compass is not just an arrow pointing north—it is part of an orientation system that allows us to understand our position in space.

We want to identify these poles in photography and ultimately obtain a tool for determining the right paths in processing images.

But isn't it possible to adjust an image without understanding the broader interconnections in photography? Of course it is!

For example, a photo artist proceeding from their own vision and sensory perception can reach their goal even without such understanding. However, looking ahead, I will say that even this approach will find its place in our system.

Nor is such an understanding required from a retoucher making changes in accordance with a given assignment. Their task in this case is to answer the question “how?” rather than ask themselves “why?” or “what for?”

One can do without it—those eager to take a shortcut on their creative journey. After all, there is no need to spend time and effort finding one's own path in processing when a well-trodden highway runs right alongside. To travel on it, one doesn't need a compass—one can simply follow the traffic. It is enough only to identify the genre of the image and repeat one of the commonly found visual styles within it.

But for those caught between these extremes, understanding photography as a system proves most useful and fascinating. For those who through processing want to convey what is embedded precisely in their own photographs, for those who search and question.

But why not employ as such a system a conception of photography as a collection of genres? Alas, genres only divide photography's body into separate parts by purpose or by the content of the images, saying little about it as a whole.

This division is merely conventional and does not constitute a unified system. You will find no two parameters that, when gradually altered, would cause an image to predictably shift from one genre category to another and back again.

Photographic genres rather resemble different color libraries—one containing printing inks, another textile dyes, a third automotive finishes. The colors within these libraries may well overlap, yet they are organized in each according to different principles.

Yet despite the various ways their internal organization can take, all of them can be described within a single coordinate system of a broader color space.

In much the same way, a unified photographic space brings together within itself photographs of completely different genres, despite their unique characteristics and established techniques.

Moreover, similar spaces can be seen in other forms of visual art—each with its own boundaries and with its own reach. In some zones they will overlap each other, in others they extend beyond the common limits.

If we look even broader, we will be able to see how the unified space of art will, in much the same way, intersect with the spaces of science, religion, and ethics, forming a unified field of culture.

So what, then, lies at the foundation of photography's unified space? What conceivable bond could connect an image of a snow leopard captured by an automated camera, a studio portrait session, and a photograph of a sunset?

The only thing that will unite these utterly dissimilar images is the very process of interaction among photography's constituent parts.

The number of elements in this interaction will vary from genre to genre and from image to image, yet the composition is quite varied.

Among them are the camera, printing technology, and people of various photographic specialties.

The functions these elements perform can vary from image to image—sometimes duplicated, sometimes absent, sometimes transferred from one element to another.

But are there among them absolutely essential elements, the elimination of which would cause photography to cease being itself?

To uncover these essential elements, we might attempt to apply a reductive method. Even the most cursory analysis reveals elements that remain present in all photographs.

The first of these is the subject of photography.

It may be either a physical object before the camera, or an immaterial concept that it expresses. Either an empty expanse of space toward which the lens is aimed, or time itself. Invisible lines of interaction, or visible ones created by contrast between light and shadow. Be it what it may, photography cannot exist without its subject.

The second is the photographer and their camera.

Their relationship is one of the most contested topics in photography, studied from different angles by Roland Barthes, Susan Sontag, and Walter Benjamin. Yet the most pertinent perspective on the relationship between camera and photographer was articulated by Vilém Flusser in the monograph Towards a Philosophy of Photography. Today, with the ubiquitous integration of AI in both cameras and processing software, the questions he raised are becoming extraordinarily relevant.

Operating within the principle of “reducing entities,” we shall regard the photographer and camera as a dual system, whose shifting roles we will explore in greater detail below.

The third, and in some sense the key element of photography is the viewer. To understand this, let us examine a pair of examples.

In recent years, two trends have converged. On one hand, the automation of photography in astrophotography, scientific fields, and surveillance systems. On the other, regular attacks by ransomware viruses on company and research institution servers, aimed at extorting ransom for data decryption.

Medium and small organizations, unlike large ones, lack sophisticated IT departments. Data backups are not always created regularly, and modest budgets often prevent them from paying ransom to hackers even when important information is encrypted.

As a result of such attacks, sometimes interesting artifacts arise: images captured without human participation and encrypted before anyone could view them. Should the decision be made not to pay the ransom, they will never be seen by anyone.

Think about it: can you call them photographs?

Not files, not encrypted information, not binary code, but photographs?

Without viewing them, we cannot judge either the captured subjects or the composition of the frame—only the very fact that data has been recorded as a file. Whether these encrypted files even contain photographs or merely white noise remains unknown until they are decrypted and viewed.

Many have encountered another such example through personal experience. It happened to me not long before writing these lines.

A couple of months ago, I saw a beautiful city landscape at sunset. Taking out my camera, I quickly adjusted the shooting parameters based on three test photographs. Unfortunately, directly in front of me stood tourists I could not get around, and the sun was setting quickly.

Afraid of missing the moment, I raised my camera above my head and shot blind, dozens of variations of the angle.

I was in a hurry, and there was no time to review the photographs. Nor was there any in the evening, so I simply copied the memory card to my computer and for a long time forgot about it.

Stumbling upon it recently, I finally decided to see what I had gotten. But for some inexplicable reason, the files would not open in any program. Yet both their names and their file sizes in megabytes were exactly the same as any other files shot by my camera. I understood the reason for this only when I thought to open one of the files in a text editor.

The files were not empty; instead, they had been meticulously filled with null values. All information in them had been completely destroyed, and it was theoretically impossible to recover it. Nor could I recover this from the memory card that had been used and overwritten many times already.

Consider whether you regard the encrypted files from the first example as more “photographic” than the files with deleted information from the second?

This seems counterintuitive, but the presence of information in the encrypted files in the first case, with no possibility of viewing it, is no different from its complete absence in the second. Yet in the second example, I did in fact view three files during the shooting process.

Yes, they were lost during recording, card extraction, or problems during copying. But even the single fact of their having been viewed makes them, though destroyed, photographs nonetheless.

Photography is neither information nor a file nor a print. It is the very experience of their visual perception.

Without a viewer, photography cannot become itself—it is meaningless. All its meanings are born in the moment of perception by a specific person, drawn from their life experience, knowledge, and context.

Photography is a door that each viewer, unlocking it with their own key, steps out into their own garden.

And one more observation can be made when we examine the elements of photographic interaction. Structurally, they prove to be very similar to the elements of the camera itself.

The role of the subject remains unchanged. The photographer and camera perform the role of the lens, focusing light and transmitting it for recording. And the viewer assumes the role of the sensor and recording device.

Without a viewer, photography would resemble a camera without loaded film or an inserted memory card. One could select a subject, compose the frame, press the shutter button. These actions would be externally completely indistinguishable from the act of photographing. But only no photographs would emerge from their result.

As for the other conclusions that can be drawn from recognizing the similarity between photography and the camera, I invite you to discover them yourselves, through the lens of your own worldview.

Thus, the primary elements of photographic interaction are before us. We see that they are not static and are in constant mutual motion.

But how can the variation of merely three fundamental elements create all that diversity of genres and styles that photography encompasses?

It is precisely this question we shall address by examining and analyzing the extreme states of our photographic system in the chapters ahead.

Chapter 3: Objective Photography

Chapter 3

Objective Photography

Another benefit of this analogy between the photographic interaction model and color space is the possibility of its visualisation in the form of a simple diagram.

Imagine a triangle whose corners are colored in the most intensely saturated primary colors—red, green, and blue. These very primary colors will be encountered by you extremely rarely. But only by describing them can you obtain, through their mixture and attenuation, all the other colors that surround you in everyday life.

Our photographic interaction model can similarly be visualized as such a triangle. Its corners will likewise be occupied by certain extreme states, which very rarely encounter us in their pure form in actual practice. Yet their understanding is absolutely necessary so that we can determine where between them an actual photograph will occupy its position.

You should understand that the analogy I am employing between color space and photographic interaction is quite conditional. And their extreme points will be structured quite differently.

As we established in the previous chapter, we cannot fully eliminate any of the three fundamental elements of photographic interaction. Instead, to achieve such extreme states, one of them must be maximally diminished.

Let us examine the first of these extreme states. Throughout this book, we shall refer to it, by convention, as objective photography.

In objective photography, the role of the photographer and their camera is reduced to a minimum. Such a photograph will convey the subject as closely as possible to what a viewer could see, standing in the photographer's position with a camera.

I would position objective photography at the top corner of our triangle, to underscore its significance.

And if I had to choose a color for its representation, I would choose green. It is in the green part of the spectrum that our vision is most sensitive to tonal nuances, and it is green that contributes most substantially to our color perception.

Most photographs we encounter will strive toward this vertex. This is precisely why we must comprehend in detail how such an image will appear in its extreme form.

I find it important to draw your attention to several characteristics of objective photography.

First, maximum approximation to what a viewer would see will not be identical to the actual process of their visual perception. There is no way to overcome the limitations imposed by the photograph itself.

The image will be two-dimensional; the frame will constrain the visible field; we will perceive only a single frozen instant; we will be unable to sense the warmth, scent, or sound emanating from the subject. Objective photography is the most accurate reflection of reality, but within the limits that photography allows.

Second, one must understand the difference between the image that falls on the viewer's retina and the image that arises in their brain.

The static image formed by the eye on the retina is highly imperfect. Our zone of sharp vision is extraordinarily narrow. But we do not notice this because looking at an object is not a single photograph, but rather a fragment of video. We usually do not even realize that the eye rapidly “scans” the scene before us, focusing on different elements of it. From these fragments, the brain assembles a composite image.

Moreover, part of the familiar objects we believe we have seen, the brain reconstructs from short-term memory or life experience. We look with our eyes, but we see with our brain.

Third, this image created by the brain will be perceived differently by each viewer, depending on their life experience, knowledge, and context. Perception of a photograph is an active process of constructing reality, not merely passive “reception of information.”

Objective photography is the creation of the most accurate possible “copy of reality” for subsequent perception by the viewer. And the aforementioned reasons impose rather strict limitations on such an image.

Before drawing any conclusions about objective photography, I propose that we examine the primary parameters of such an image. I will present their description not as a guide for adjusting your photographs, but so that you better understand what such an image looks like.

But at this moment I ask you to actively engage with the reading. After reading each point, ask yourself: how far are your photographs from this “maximum possible truth”? And can you answer yourself the question “why?” So:

Lens Focal Length: The human angle of sharp vision is approximately 40–60°. This corresponds to lenses with a focal length of 43 to 50mm on a full-frame sensor. A 50mm lens is commonly called a “normal lens”.

Aspect Ratio: Research shows that aspect ratios between 3:2 and 4:3 are closest to human vision. The 3:2 aspect ratio, common in digital SLRs and mirrorless cameras, is somewhat wider than human perception, while the 4:3 aspect ratio, more frequently found in medium-format and compact cameras, corresponds to it most closely.

Horizon Line: Always parallel to the top and bottom of the frame. Our brain forms and maintains a long-term internal model of space in which up and down are defined independently of our body's position. The horizon remains horizontal for us, even if we lie on our side.

Camera Position and Viewing Angle: They are rigidly determined by the position of the viewer's eyes relative to the subject. In objective photography, the use of camera angle as a creative technique is inadmissible.

Chromatic and Spherical Aberrations: Like any individual optical characteristics of a lens, these must either be absent or corrected during post-processing in objective photography.

Depth of Field: Maximum possible. Our brain actively suppresses the perception of blur, reconstructing familiar objects from memory or lived experience. Although we do perceive some unfamiliar or unrecognized objects as blurred, their placement bears no relation to the lens's focal point. Thus, overall maximum sharpness across the entire frame, while not identical to what the viewer perceives, comes closest to it.

Frame Composition: The primary subject should be located at the center of the frame. This most accurately conveys the alignment between head direction and gaze direction. This alignment evolved to compensate for energy expenditure, coordinate different sensory inputs, and accommodate our narrow angle of sharp vision. If the position of a new object of attention differs by as little as 15–25 degrees from the previous one, we automatically turn our head and look at it directly.

White Balance: In objective photography, it is corrected based on the context of the photograph and the duration of the viewer's contact with the subject. Our brain adapts to the color of illumination at approximately the same rate, regardless of its hue.

When lighting changes, the brain compensates halfway within 40–100 milliseconds. However, complete adaptation takes more than a minute, which must be considered when adjusting the photograph.

For example, lighting indoors with incandescent bulbs, which we experience for an entire evening, will be perceived by us as neutral. But a passerby glancing at it through a window will see it flooded with saturated yellow-orange light. This occurs because of the brief perception time and the bluish evening light to which the passerby's vision has already adapted.

Use of white balance alteration as a creative technique in objective photography is likewise inadmissible.

Color Profile: Realistic, constructed on the basis of a photograph of a standard color reference chart to compensate for the camera's and lighting's particular characteristics.

Distortion of color or saturation (as well as any other parameters) for the sake of greater expressiveness is not permitted in objective photography.

Dynamic Range: Maximum possible. Scenes in the world around us can span a range exceeding 26 exposure stops.

According to research, our eye, depending on conditions, can instantaneously perceive between 6.5 and 14 stops. However, as the eye scans across a scene, it gradually adapts the pupil size, and the brain combines details perceived during different fixations into a single image with a range exceeding 20 stops.

Unfortunately, here we encounter the imperfection of technology and the impossibility of reconciling the last two listed parameters in actual photography—and here is why.

We can create an image with an extended dynamic range, or HDR, by blending photographs taken at different exposures. A precise camera color profile is created from a single RAW image and can only be applied to another single image.

Building a color profile based on a standard color reference chart in HDR is impossible, since in the different exposures the color samples are either underexposed or overexposed.

This means we must choose which parameter to sacrifice.

Either we create an HDR image whose colors will not accurately correspond to reality. Though we can approximate the desired colors through manual correction, they will nevertheless remain “fabricated”, which contradicts the ideal of objective photography.

Or we create a single image with a precise color profile. However, the dynamic range of RAW files from modern cameras is 12–15 stops, which will be insufficient to capture scenes with highly contrasty lighting.

Or we create a series of single images with color profiles and combine portions of them into a unified image in a graphics editor. But this would mean our active creative participation in constructing the image, which would, once again, distance us from objective photography.

Perhaps technological advances will overcome these limitations, but for now they must be taken into account.

I believe that even this enumeration of the most fundamental parameters of objective photography allows you to imagine such an image. And I would encourage you, at least once in your life, to independently attempt creating an image as close to it as possible.

Examine it closely, listening to yourself. What do you feel in this moment?

Are you bored?

Does your gaze lack movement?

Does the frame composition fail to suggest a direction, leaving you feeling irritated?

Do you crave more color, contrast, accents? Intense emotions and experiences?

Does your sense of beauty demand a more aesthetic composition?

Does the unfamiliar silence and the absence of any coherent narrative trouble you?

Does the photograph fail to entertain you with unusual angles and authorial discoveries?

Viewing such an image is as strange and unfamiliar as catching your reflection on a cold Monday morning—or hearing your own voice amplified through speakers in a large hall.

With the photographer and camera having nearly vanished in objective photography, the viewer's voice begins to sound loudly and clearly—in this case, your own voice.

No other form of photography, as you will discover later, allows you to manifest your own beliefs, aspirations, and desires in such measure. To reveal them and make a decision about what you wish to photograph next.

You will not create such images except for purposes of self-analysis.

In reality, no photograph can exist that fully corresponds to all the requirements of objective photography. It is merely a theoretical extreme point on the Photography Triangle. Even a passport photo would be more conventional and expressive.

Such an image could be taken by you, me, or anyone else—there is no personal signature of the author in it.

You will forget this photograph the moment you stop looking at it. Only the image of the object or scene will remain in your mind.

Like distilled water, it is extremely pure, but it has no flavor and will not quench your thirst for beauty.

Objective photography is not an ideal to which you will aspire. It is a zero point of reference—a baseline from which you can push off to create something new, to tell stories, and to embody in your photographs what you wish to convey.

It will depend solely on you how far from it you venture—but like a lighthouse, it is always useful to keep it in your field of vision. Should you face the task of conveying objective reality, the parameters of your image that were listed above will gradually shift toward this “zero point of reference.”

Likewise, you can measure any photograph against the objective angle of the triangle by assessing how closely it approaches its “maximum possible realism.”

This analysis becomes particularly intriguing when applied to genres commonly regarded as the “mirror of reality” and the “voice of truth”—photojournalism and documentary photography.

Against this objective backdrop, you will readily notice in such photographs the manipulation of viewers through compositional techniques, the reshaping of narrative through framing, and the author's perspective on the subject or event—deliberately constructing what the viewer will accept as “witnessed with their own eyes.”

Alas, truth and truthfulness are not synonymous.

And now, having understood what the maximally realistic angle of our triangle looks like, let us turn to its other extreme condition.

In it, the photographer will fully discover their own voice, and we shall see where this leads.

Chapter 4: Subjective Photography

Chapter 4

Subjective Photography

In the second of the triangle's extreme conditions, among the three elements of photographic interaction, the role of the subject is maximally weakened. It does not disappear entirely—otherwise such a system could no longer be called photography—but becomes merely one of its auxiliary components. Its role may be even less significant than that of the light source employed during the shoot or the chosen focal length of the lens.

In this book, we will conventionally call such an extreme condition subjective photography.

If I had to choose a color to represent it, it would be red—as a symbol of active authorial position.

Subjective photography is an equally theoretical and unattainable point in reality—a vertex of our model—just as objective photography is.

The photographer in it is an embodiment of pure reason, consciously creating and seeking contact with the viewer through photographic means of expression. In this extreme condition, it would be more accurate to call him an author, but to maintain terminological consistency, we will continue to call him a photographer.

At this extreme point of the triangle, the photographer operates exclusively from logic and knows precisely what he wishes to communicate through his photograph. There is no room for either the unconscious or the incomprehensible. This is precisely why it is as inaccessible in reality as objective photography is.

The worldview from the subjective vertex of the triangle is, first and foremost, a perspective rooted in Western philosophical and scientific thought, in which defining the boundaries of consciousness and the possibility of communicating experience remain among the most crucial concerns across centuries.

Since the time of René Descartes, European philosophers have contemplated the isolation of consciousness and the possibility of communication only through the senses.

Modern science concurs. Neuropsychology confirms that each brain is isolated within its own boundaries and receives information about the world only through sensory channels. All our knowledge is formed from electrical signals originating from the sense organs.

Alas, the photographer has no way to directly convey his intention to the viewer.

True, neurointerface technologies enabling information transfer from one brain to another via specialized equipment have been under active development recently. Claims of up to 90% transmission success and the creation of “brain networks” for collaborative problem-solving have been made.

Yet if you examine the details, you find that these primarily involve the transmission of rudimentary binary messages (yes/no, start/stop) or the transfer of basic sensory perceptions and motor commands (up/down, hand movement). Attempts are also being made to recognize and transmit fundamental emotions (fear, joy, anger, surprise, interest, anxiety). But these are transmitted merely as a kind of “emotional vector” sensation capable of inducing a similar state in the recipient.

As you can understand, this very limited toolkit cannot convey an idea, a concept, or the feeling of love. Even if you receive a signal through a neurointerface and experience a sense of fear—it will be your fear. You know no more about how it feels to another person than you would from reading the word “fear” or looking at someone else's frightened face.

These technologies are powerless to overcome the distance between consciousnesses and can only narrow it.

The primary goal of subjective photography is to attempt to bridge this gap between photographer and viewer using photography's expressive language.

Yet the viewer need not be someone else. This “other” can be the photographer himself at the moment of viewing his own photograph. By first focusing and articulating his thoughts and experiences in the form of a photograph, then deciphering them as a message from his “past self,” the photographer can use subjective photography as an effective tool for shaping a “new self.” In this case, the goal of bridging the gap between consciousnesses does not disappear but transforms into inner work toward this “future self.”

To bridge this gap between photographer and viewer, one of the most crucial questions in subjective photography becomes the choice of language for transmitting the photographic message. And if you say that this language will be “the language of photography,” I would ask you to speak something in Cyrillic or Latin script.

A lengthy catalog of dozens of elements—encompassing the choice of subject, technical and geometric considerations, work with light and color, compositional techniques, and much else—bears greater resemblance to an alphabet than to a language.

Just as letters from a single alphabet can be employed across numerous natural languages, so too can a unified photographic alphabet enable the creation of countless photographic languages composed of them.

Like natural languages, photographic ones can be more or less widespread, rare, or dead.

Yet in order to select or develop a new language for transmitting one's photographic message, the photographer must first understand to which viewers he is addressing it, and which languages they are capable of comprehending.

For instance, a photographer may set as his goal to make his photographic language comprehensible to the majority of viewers. This desire to be understood by all will necessitate either the choice of the most widespread language or the narrowing of its vocabulary to elements uniformly perceived by the majority of people.

One such example of a most widespread photographic language can be seen in the style of images automatically applied when photographing with a modern smartphone. In such images you will find a combination of expanded tonal range, slightly elevated contrast, saturation, and sharpness. Smoothed pink skin and blurred backgrounds in portraits. Intensified colors of sky and foliage in landscapes. I imagine you understand what I am describing, as this visual style—combined with two or three basic compositional techniques and an overall “positive” tenor—constantly confronts you in media, social networks, and advertising.

You may or may not like such images, but this photographic language is the most widespread in the contemporary world. Though it is neither the only one nor native to viewers, the majority of them will nevertheless be able to perceive and comprehend the message conveyed through it. And if you wish to be understood by the majority, you should speak a language they can comprehend.

There is another way to make your image comprehensible to the maximum number of viewers. For this, you must develop your photographic language based on compositional techniques that are perceived at a biological level by all people, regardless of their culture or education.

For several decades now, psychologists and neurophysiologists have been conducting active research into the mechanisms of physiological perception. Some studies have included not only humans but other primates as well, demonstrating the evolutionary roots of at least part of what we consider beautiful or harmonious.

Here are some such biologically perceived elements of an image:

Associations of warm and cold colors with temperature: Research demonstrates that the connection between temperature and “warm” and “cold” colors is encoded already at the retinal level, which directly influences our physiological responses. We physically perceive red as warmer and blue as colder.

For example, when assessing room temperature, participants perceived it several degrees higher in a room painted red than when assessing temperature in a room with blue walls.

Left-side preference: Experiments involving humans, primates, birds, and mice demonstrate that the left visual field and the corresponding left side of the frame receive greater attention when perceiving an image. This left-side preference in image perception is based on the leading role of the right hemisphere in processing what we see.

For people who read left-to-right, this effect is additionally reinforced by learning and habit, yet even those who read right-to-left predominantly begin perceiving images from the left side.

A composition in which eye movement is intended to proceed from left to right will be more natural for the majority of viewers, regardless of their training or cultural specifics.

Physiology of atmospheric perspective: Two effects exerted the most profound influence on the evolution of our ancestors—longitudinal chromatic aberration in the eye and evolutionary adaptation to atmospheric light scattering.

The first consequence of this is that blue objects are perceived as more distant, while red ones are perceived as closer. This effect persists even when perceiving a single color against a neutral background: for example, orange on a white background appears nearer.

A second consequence emerges in the perception of saturation as information about scene depth. At equal brightness, more saturated colors feel closer, while less saturated colors appear more distant.

The maximum effect of “saturation-depth” is perceived in gradations of red against a neutral background.

A third consequence is that the viewer perceives high-contrast objects as positioned closer, while low-contrast objects appear more distant from them.

Thus, atmospheric perspective is not merely a compositional technique, but rather a reflection of the biological and evolutionary roots of our perception.

Brightness-based depth perception: In the absence of linear or atmospheric perspective, the brain switches to local brightness differences. Within a single object, darker regions will be perceived as more distant, while lighter regions will be perceived as protruding forward. Accordingly, on a monochromatic background, the viewer will consider a brighter object closer or higher than the others.

Biologically justified contour accentuation: When perceiving complex images, our eye responds unequally to different levels of detail. We are particularly attuned to contrasts in mid-sized details—not very large blobs or very minute specifics, but something intermediate, such as faces, figures, or objects. The stronger the contrast of these mid-range contours, the more noticeably they will “stand out” forward and appear closer.

You can use this effect both to accentuate the object you need in the frame and to reduce contour contrast of distracting objects.

This will operate at the biological level and redirect attention to the parts of the frame you need.

Pattern pleasingness: Research involving humans and primates has demonstrated that primary and secondary visual cortices contain neurons tuned to rapid detection and “reassurance” upon encountering repeated elements. Repetition signals safety, stability, and the absence of hidden threats. In photography, one can use the repetition of identical elements to organize the frame and calm the viewer. Conversely, one can disrupt rhythm to attract attention to that part of the image.

Attractiveness of symmetry: Not only humans, but other primates as well, prefer more symmetrical faces to less symmetrical ones. This is confirmed by activation of neurons in the visual cortex, which points to the evolutionary causes of this.

However, one should not attempt to make a model's face absolutely symmetrical: research shows that ideal symmetry is not perceived as an ideal of beauty. On the contrary, a touch of natural asymmetry makes the appearance more attractive than absolute symmetry.

Leading lines: Experiments reveal that neurons responding to light become activated when leading lines in a static image create a sense of motion. People from different cultures, without any training in art or photography, and even preschool children automatically follow their gaze along leading lines in the frame.

Non-biological nature of the golden ratio: Research on the perception of facial harmony has shown that optimal proportions preferred by viewers are 36% for the distance from eyes to mouth relative to face length, and 46% for interpupillary distance relative to face width. That is, faces perceived as beautiful turn out to be narrower than the golden ratio requires.

Another study confirmed that fewer than half of participants perceive golden ratio proportions as most attractive. The golden ratio as an “ideal of beauty” does not arise from the biological mechanisms of our perception. And if you harmonize facial proportions during retouching, consider instead these experimentally determined ratios.

This is far from an exhaustive list, but bear in mind that many contemporary studies are conducted on relatively small samples. However, results are constantly being refined, and increasingly reliable and accurate data about the biological nature of these and other compositional techniques continue to emerge.

By grounding your photographic language in them, you will render your images comprehensible to viewers regardless of their age, region, culture, or education.

Regional differences in image perception also exist, comparable to widespread national languages.

And while serious and consistent research on differences between Northern and Southern perception is extremely scarce, differences between Eastern and Western perception are well-studied and quite substantial.

Differences in social organization, artistic traditions, and architecture result in distinctive perceptual characteristics among people of Asian and European cultures. Research identifies several key differences:

Western viewers expect contrastive emphasis of the central element and prefer minimalist backgrounds. Asian viewers, by contrast, devote greater attention to overall frame harmony, value detailed, multi-layered backgrounds, and perceive the general interrelationships between elements. They are more responsive to symmetry and balance among image components.

These differences are reinforced by research on visual organization: Europeans tend to seek clear structure and simplicity in images, whereas Chinese participants are more tolerant of visual diversity, perceiving complexity as part of aesthetics.

However, cultural boundaries do not align with state borders. For instance, in an international study of perception of complex scenes, substantial differences were identified between individuals from Asian and Western countries. Yet participants from Eastern and Western Turkey, influenced by different cultural spheres, demonstrated significant divergence in perception.

Yet the most striking differences we see emerge in differences in the meanings of colors in Asian and Western countries.

In European culture, white is traditionally associated with purity and is used as the color of wedding dress, whereas in Asia, by contrast, it is the color of mourning, worn at funerals. Yellow in Asia symbolizes imperial authority and sanctity, while in Europe it signifies jealousy or betrayal. Red in Asia represents luck, joy, and prosperity. For Europeans, however, it evokes associations not only with danger but also with passion.

However, in the contemporary world, many rituals and practices often intersect with one another. One can readily encounter Asian brides dressed in white—due to globalization, wedding fashion is beginning to blend cultural traditions. One or another representative of Western or Eastern culture may adhere to the norms of the opposite region as a result of education, personal preference, or professional activity.

Despite the traditional top-to-bottom reading direction in China, horizontal reading among regional representatives proved faster and yielded better retention. Contemporary Asian viewers have adapted to horizontal reading through internet use, education, and work.

The conclusion you can draw from this when formulating your image's “photographic language” is: avoid the naive categorization of people into “Asians” and “Europeans.” Regional particularities of perception are an objective fact, and they will influence people from the corresponding region to varying degrees. But beyond this, there are also other factors that will exert no less influence.

Just as you will easily identify in “photographic language” analogs of regional languages or dialects. In various religious, professional, or social communities you will find their own “visual language”—gestures, angles, color combinations, iconic objects comprehensible only to the in-group.

Enumerating these visual codes for each such group would be counterproductive and would divert this book from its primary focus. However, let me note—as someone who has photographed subcultures—that this “visual language” is quite subtle. The smaller such a group, the more critical it becomes for its members that you understand and deploy this language precisely. How much you, as a photographer, deserve their trust.

When formulating your message for a closed community, you must exercise considerably greater care in the choice of compositional techniques than when shooting for generic “Europeans” or “men”.

A “photographic language” comprehensible to only a single specific person is also possible. Constructed from signs known only to the two of you, it will resemble a secret message between intimates.

Yet deliberate “nonsense,” constructed as a thoughtful edifice, can convey meaning to the viewer, becoming the photographic analog of Oberiu poetry or Lewis Carroll's verse language.

Many techniques employed by absurdists can be transposed onto the language of photography. Logical rupture might be conveyed through sharp deviation from the rule of thirds and compositional voids. Puns might be achieved through double exposure and layering of objects. Repetitions and variations—through discordant rhythms of patterns. Paradoxes—through clashing colors. The transition from reality to the grotesque—through the mixture of documentary and advertising styles. An open ending—through unexpected framing that does not tell a complete story.

Moreover, it is precisely in subjective photography, grounded in logic and reason, that this acquires special significance. If your goal is to disrupt the viewer's automatic perception rooted in familiar templates, to compel them to regard the structure of order from an unfamiliar angle, then “visual absurdism” can become a powerful and effective language.

Another conclusion you can draw from the similarity between photographic language and natural languages is an understanding of the finiteness of the “photographic sentence.”

Photography, like a sentence, has a beginning and an end. It is impossible to convey an infinite internal monologue through the means of the frame. This finiteness of the photographic phrase inevitably leads to incompleteness in the meanings transmitted, just as the photograph's frame limits itself to a single chosen fragment of reality. Since the goal of subjective photography is to convey thought to the viewer as precisely as possible, this requires a strict limitation of the “length” of visual utterance.

All of the above leads us to the conclusion that even in an ideal situation, mutual understanding between photographer and viewer will be possible only when all the aforementioned factors converge.

The photographer's “circle of expression” is not ideal. The message they wish to convey to the viewer will be incomplete due to the limitation of its length and inevitable inaccuracies in translation to the chosen photographic language.

The viewer's “circle of perception” is similarly imperfect. It resides at the intersection of the biological, cultural, and social languages they know, are able to understand, or can perceive.

Only in the zone of intersection between the photographer's circle of expression and the viewer's circle of perception can successful communication between them occur.

Nevertheless, in any photograph there will always be present the phenomenon of “hidden meanings.”

Since the viewer is a full co-author of the image, the perception of compositional elements and framing by a viewer with a different “circle of perception” can lead to their decoding in a different manner, or even in a diametrically opposite way.

For instance, photographs created with the aim of glorifying a particular idea are often perceived by its opponents with precise opposition. And images created in the language of a small community, containing for its participants a profound meaning, may appear absurd or aesthetically unappealing to those outside it.

The miracle of photography lies precisely in this: it is a bottomless “box of meanings,” containing far more than the photographer intended or any particular viewer is capable of perceiving.

“Hidden meanings” are inevitable, but if the photographer has failed to formulate their message clearly—then these hidden meanings become the primary content of their image.

As for the technical aspects of subjective photography, note that the sole criterion of “correctness” or “incorrectness” of image parameters lies in how effectively they communicate the intention to the chosen viewer. Whether they correspond to the realistic criteria of objective photography is entirely irrelevant.

What would be considered distortions in objective photography becomes a working tool in subjective photography and will be welcomed precisely if it brings the image closer to the photographer's intention.

The defining characteristic of subjective photography is not its departure from realism or how “creative” a message it conveys. Yes, the photographer can use it to communicate a concept, tell a story, or express their position. But it can equally be their questions to themselves, their memories, or their internal monologue.

The essential feature of subjective photography is the voice of the photographer, which resonates loudest here. And it resounds precisely in the language of photography itself.

Have you not noticed at contemporary photography exhibitions that you often spend more time reading than visually perceiving images? Photographs that are hardly expressive, accompanied by five pages of conceptual description, appear quite frequently there.

The language of these works communicates its message to a select few connoisseurs. Yet the rarefied nature of this language contradicts the commercial imperative to present these images to viewers, the majority of whom do not command such a language. They require textual guidance—explaining what one should think about these images, lest they accidentally mistake them for what they might appear to be from the viewers' “circle of perception.”

And in each such case, one is left only to speculate: did the photographer fail to convey their thought through photographic means alone, or are you simply not their audience—and have no place in that gallery.

Photography, as a visual art form, possesses an extensive arsenal of expressive techniques and is fully capable of communicating with viewers in its own language rather than in the language of literature, video, or augmented reality. If we do venture beyond its boundaries—this is entirely legitimate, but we must be conscious of this choice and understand precisely why we are making it.

And now, having thoroughly explored what the subjective angle of our triangle represents, let us journey toward its third “extreme condition.”

And the closer we approach it, the quieter the author's insistent voice will become.

Chapter 5: Perceptual Photography

Chapter 5

Perceptual Photography

Let us explore what the third vertex of the Photography Triangle reveals through the interaction of its constituent elements. The viewer's role is minimized to its extreme here. It would be more accurate to call them a co-viewer—underscoring the subordinate nature of their participation.

The primary viewer here becomes the photographer themselves, and the presence of an external viewer is not obligatory, though it remains possible.

We shall conventionally refer to such an extreme condition of the Photography Triangle as perceptual photography.

And if I had to choose a color to represent it, it would be blue—symbolizing silence and perception.

The word “viewer” itself, in the context of perceptual photography, does not convey their true role. It is more accurately described by the Greek theōrós, the Latin contemplator, or the Russian созерцатель. Yet to maintain consistent terminology across all chapters, we shall continue to use the term viewer, keeping in mind that we invest it with precisely these meanings.

Like the previous vertices of the Photography Triangle, perceptual photography is equally a theoretical extreme point. Yet while we could not reach the point of objective photography due to technical limitations, nor the point of subjective photography due to the nature of human psychology, we cannot achieve the point of perceptual photography because there is nothing in it.

Perceptual photography is the extreme point to which we can advance within photography by directing our gaze beyond the boundaries of the triangle. It is, first and foremost, the very direction of the viewer's gaze itself.

This direction will be the same for everyone, but what their gaze discovers there—will be visible to them alone.

Depending on the individual and their worldview, this could be the spiritual conceptions of their faith as well as the realm of the unconscious. As visions of the absolute, as well as visions of emptiness.

There exists no universal perspective on what lies in the direction of this gaze, nor agreement on whether it is directed inward or outward. As a rule, discussion of these perspectives proves meaningless and leads only to discord and misunderstanding between us.

Both the goal as well as the map by which you follow in this direction can only be your own. Even if you join one of the groups pursuing the same destination, no one can chart your path for you, for we each occupy different points on the map.

Yet this does not mean that the subjective nature of our perception and differences in our perspectives render communication between us impossible. Viewing photography as interaction allows us to discuss the path itself and exchange experience, regardless of the goal of our journey. To discuss what equipment best suits particular conditions, how not to freeze or burn, and how to breathe properly to maintain your pace on the route.

Within the field of photography, you cannot travel further along this route beyond the extreme point of perceptual photography. Whether you should continue it further by stepping beyond this boundary, or remain within it—each will decide for themselves.

For the reasons listed above, speaking about perceptual photography is exceedingly difficult. Silence dissolves the moment you attempt to describe it, and returns only when you fall silent.

Yet within the bounds of text, all I can do is select analogies and metaphors and hope that you can relate them to your own experience. I ask your forgiveness in advance for their inexactness and incompleteness.

I will attempt to use for its description a sufficiently meaningless phrase: “the wordless voice of this moment.” That very instantly appearing sensation which stands both behind the subject of the photograph and behind compositional devices and behind the author's narrative.

In actual photographs, the objective, subjective, and perceptual elements are intermingled. To separate the influence on the viewer of biologically perceived aspects of the image from the perceptual reception of what stands behind them is exceedingly difficult. Nevertheless, I believe that each of you has experienced at least once, upon looking at a photograph, a certain sensation that appeared before you had time to become conscious of it.

It can be very “loud”, like a clap of thunder, and perceived by many viewers. Some describe it as a “sharp flash”, others as a “chill down the spine”, still others as “butterflies in the stomach”. If you have ever experienced such a “short circuit” while viewing a photograph, then you will understand what I am saying.

This wordless voice of the moment will not depend on either the genre of photography or its subject. You may encounter it in your own work or someone else's, in a masterpiece or in a child's first photograph.

Yet in other cases, it may be quiet, barely perceptible, and audible only to you. You may find yourself transfixed by an outwardly unremarkable photograph that moves no one else. And try as you might to analyze it, you will find nothing noteworthy in either its subject matter or its composition.

The wordless voice of the moment is not a message from the subject of the photograph—it has neither content nor duration. It's something that triggers a personal stream of associations and sensations in each viewer, but isn't the sensation itself. Perhaps you've experienced it yourself in series of photographs with a repeating composition.

For instance, in a series of portraits of a model with identical pose, composition, and lighting, each photograph will possess a different “loudness” of this voice. Yet the variations in the frame's composition will be minimal, and you will not be able to claim that the composition itself is the cause of this difference in perception.

Or a landscape captured casually on a smartphone may “sound” distinctly and piercingly, yet have insufficient resolution. In attempts to recapture it in better quality on a proper camera, you may spend years returning to that same location. Recreating everything down to the pixel—matching the weather and the date—yet all proves futile. Despite their outward similarity, these photographs “sound” completely different. Again, it's not a matter of composition, but something else entirely.

Or the camera unintentionally captures a fragment of a dancing couple, and in the gesture of the arm embracing the partner's waist, there emerges care, strength, and a story of a man and a woman. If the shot turns out of focus, you may spend the entire evening trying to reshoot it with exactly the same angle, lighting, and couple. But all proves futile. Despite their apparent indistinguishability, the photograph contains only a man's hand resting on a woman's lower back.

In most cases, this wordless voice of the moment proves quiet enough to be nearly completely drowned out by the psyche's reaction to familiar realistic objects or by the author's loud voice.

Moving toward perceptual photography is a practice of conscious, active perception of the image at every stage: from the moment of capture through final selection and color correction.

The goal of perceptual photography is to allow the “voice of the moment” to manifest and be heard.

Yet it is entirely possible that your emotional response to it will not be positive. You do not know beforehand what you will find beyond the doors of perception.

The process of active perception can bring not only joy, love, or peace, but nausea, fear, or disgust. Your own photographs may make you feel ill or repulsed.

You can simply delete such “negative” photographs or curate conditions in which you produce exclusively “positive” frames. Or you can attempt to understand them and begin changing something within yourself, tracking the transformation through new photographs.

Perceptual photography can become a powerful instrument for inner transformation. But one must proceed with great caution on this path. For the emergence of schizophrenic symptoms could also be called a transformation—though hardly a desirable one.

The Photography Triangle is merely a compass, not a map or a magnetic pole. It allows one to judge only the presence of change, not the quality of that change.

To change in the right direction, you require an external frame of reference that lies beyond the boundaries of photography itself. I do not know whether for you this will be humanistic values, ethical principles, the tenets of your religion, or concepts of emptiness. But without such an external reference point, following the light can lead you to will-o'-the-wisps in the swamp. If you feel unstable, it is better to seek supervision or counsel from a psychologist, priest, or teacher—depending on your convictions or worldview.

Personally, I cannot help you choose your own reference points, nor can I guide you in understanding what lies beyond the boundaries of the triangle. But I can share my thoughts on the technical aspects of such photography and on processing its results.

If any of the approaches described below remind you of techniques familiar from other practices, applying them will help you move faster along the path of perceptual photography.

Photography demands active mental engagement. You must prepare your equipment, determine and set shooting parameters, arrange studio lighting, position yourself relative to the sun—and so forth. All of this requires active mental work, which by momentum continues during the act of shooting itself.

Separate the preparation phase from the act of shooting itself, so that the ceaseless inner monologue does not drown out the wordless voice of the moment.

Once you have completed all preparatory work, silence notifications on your phone, calm your breathing and emotions. Endeavor to minimize distracting factors beforehand. If some cannot be changed, acknowledge them to yourself, but do not dwell on them.

It is worth establishing a physical marker that signals to your body that you have entered “shooting mode.” This action should be practical both when holding the camera and during post-processing work at your computer.

Experiment with shifting your weight from one side of your body to the other, movements of your shoulders or head, or stretching. Most importantly, this marker should align with your personal shooting style and remain consistently the same.

During shooting, endeavor not to become emotionally invested. Track your internal response to changes within the frame rather than inventing “voices from without.” Try during the moment of shooting to feel the pressure of your finger on the shutter button, the weight of the camera, and your own pressure against the ground.

If you need to quickly return to camera settings and active mental engagement, try using the same physical marker to switch back into “logic mode.”

Separate the shooting phase from the editing phase as much as time permits. Likely, you will develop preferences for certain shots over others while shooting. If your work is not commercial, set the material aside without detailed examination for a day, a week, or a month. Those initial impressions, unless reinforced by careful viewing, will fade—and will not hinder you in your selection.

Having prepared to view your images, use your physical marker again to transition into active perception. Endeavor not to evaluate the photographs based on their composition or subject matter, and instead concentrate on the internal response that each image evokes in you.

This does not mean that some photographs were “louder” or “quieter” than others. In perceptual photography, you are not evaluating the image—you are evaluating yourself during the act of capture. Further analysis of when you successfully perceived the wordless voice of the moment and when you remained deaf to it will help you analyze yourself further. You will be able to use this analysis for continued work on yourself, tracking your progress through subsequent shoots.

Resist the urge to immediately process the selected images. To ensure your material does not become lost between shooting, selection, and processing, include the shoot type and date in your folder names.

As you begin processing the images, apply your physical marker again and transition into active perception. For me personally, the process of adjusting photographs in perceptual photography most resembles tuning a shortwave radio receiver.

Today, electromagnetic interference from consumer electronics has rendered such devices nearly obsolete in urban environments, but the experience was once common to many.

On shortwave frequencies, one could discover faint, crackling stations thousands of kilometers away. Voices speaking in languages unknown to you, or music from the East and South, carrying within it an entirely different understanding of harmony.

Listening to shortwave radio taught one to value chance: it was precisely by chance that all the most interesting and unusual stations were found. It taught precision: to tune into a station, one had to turn the dial exactly right and track the drifting frequency. It taught appreciation of the moment despite its imperfection: the understanding that a melody heard might never be heard again helped one concentrate on its beauty rather than becoming distracted by the noise of interference.

Like a radio enthusiast searching for a station, the photographer practicing perceptual photography adjusts the controls, carefully monitoring your internal response. And if adjusting a particular setting does not amplify this internal response, it is better to leave the parameter unchanged. But if that feeling suggests stepping beyond “realism,” trust it.

The resulting images may appear visually close to either objective or subjective photography. What matters is not how they will look. What matters only is whether the perceptual adjustment has made the wordless voice of the moment louder or not.

The approach to shooting and processing I describe is only a particular implementation of perceptual photography's principles. It has its own limitations and is not universal. Feel free to use those elements that align with your own practice, or develop your own. Following this direction, only through trial and error will you discover which techniques and approaches allow you to clarify your own perception.

Yet remember that perceptual photography, like the other two vertices of the triangle, is virtually unattainable. We are all subject to the pull of our desires, habits, and others' opinions. These will inevitably “draw” our images toward either objective or subjective photography.

Yet therein lies the uniqueness of perceptual photography—you don't even need to reach it. What matters is only what route has already been traveled. After all, it is simply a direction of movement, not a destination.

And so at last the entire triangle of photography lies before us. Everything we call photography finds its place between its three vertices.

Let us now try to rise above it and observe the irreconcilable struggle of the forces that compose it.

Chapter 6: A Flight Over Middle-earth

Chapter 6

A Flight Over Middle-earth

Three silhouettes of peaks rise above a vast valley. Crimson, emerald, and sapphire peaks glow unreachably in the rarefied mountain air. With each step, the path to them grows steeper, and the ascent becomes more difficult.

From atop each peak, the other two appear merely as dim dots upon the horizon. And even though you know them to be equally high and majestic, your eye will inevitably convince you of the opposite.

Climbing any of them will change both you and your worldview. You will not ascend far without embracing their “sole and only truth.” The worldviews from the other two peaks will appear to you equally alien, equidistant, and false.

From the perspective of objective photography, a photograph is the recording of reality as it exists. Objects and subjects in the frame do not carry symbolic meaning—they simply are. Their form, the measurable dimensions and boundaries they possess, is determined entirely by light and shadow acting according to the laws of optics.

A camera records wavelengths of light, not invented essences. Subjective photography merely amplifies the photographer's own ego, and perceptual photography mumbles something incoherently about “perception.”

Photography must represent reality, not construct illusions and philosophize.

Realistic photography has a definitive, measurable range of parameters, and achieving maximum proximity to this range is its very essence. Deviations from this range constitute distortion, and their deliberate introduction in subjective and perceptual photography is a lie.

From the perspective of subjective photography, a photograph is a message—one that bridges the gap between the mind of its author and that of its viewer. The creative will of the author, through photography, unites their individual “I” and those of the audience into a shared “we.”

Any changes in composition or camera settings that facilitate person-to-person communication are not merely permissible—they are essential.

Devoid of the author's voice, objective photography places redundant emphasis on technique and camera settings, reducing the photographer to a functionary of the apparatus.

Perceptual photography, on the other hand, instead of uniting people's minds through communication, merely isolates them within the capsules of private perception. Both of these approaches are dehumanizing and fundamentally mistaken.

From the perspective of perceptual photography, a photograph is but a bud that unfolds within one's inner experience in the moment of contemplation. This contemplation helps purify the inner eye of attachment to praise and illusory patterns. Photography itself—the act of capture, curation, and adjustment—is not the goal, but the path toward self-transformation through conscious perception.

Both objective and subjective photography merely perpetuate and reinforce illusion, replacing living perception with frozen dogmas.

Objective photography does so by presenting the external form of objects as their true nature, while subjective photography imposes upon the viewer meanings that are not their own through the artist's monologue.

Nothing is more distant than the worldviews from these three peaks. Nothing is more kindred than their singular purpose and unwavering resolve. Nothing is less visible than what lies beneath the clouds, in the valley at their feet.

Below the clouds, ordinary people live their lives. For them, photography is neither a destination nor a path.

Here, a student takes out their phone, captures an image of their lunch, posts it to social media. A couple of likes from friends arrive—a little dopamine dessert.

And here, the mother takes pictures day after day of how her child grows. Old, dark, and blurry shots are no less precious to her than those taken with a new mirrorless camera after she completed a photography course.

A wedding photographer follows the new seasonal “trend” in color grading—not so much a language for speaking with viewers, but a way to get more bookings.

Here, a woman, without knowing why, took a photo of a patch of light on the wall of a house. She won't delete it for a long time, and this meaningless photo will somehow bother her.

Here, in the valley, photography is simply a shot. In these and in countless other images, there will be mixed, in varying degrees, the desire to capture reality, the attempt to say something, and the vague sense of something undefined.

This intermingling and inseparability appears gray when viewed from any of the sparkling peaks.

But the valley's center is less gray and more neutral. Only at its center are all three forces of photography fully balanced. The rest of the field is woven from endless variations of realism, creativity, and the inexpressible in our photographs.

The quiet wisdom of the center lies in the ease of shifting one's position. The three extreme forces compensate for one another here, and it is precisely here that it becomes easiest to begin photographing something entirely different.

Yes, it is precisely near the valley's center that most everyday photographs settle. They are dull and monotonous, and all the divergent forces of photography are expressed in them with equal faintness.

But beside them will also settle the masterworks of photography's masters, captivating with the power and harmony contained within them. Their bright white light is that same neutrality, but at its maximum intensity.

Equally reflecting objective reality, speaking to what matters, and retaining their authentic essence—they find resonance in each person.

Here, in the valley, there is no fixed position, no understanding of “how it should be done.” The valley's truth is simple: but is it even necessary to go anywhere?

Yes, for the most part it means treading water, endlessly shooting similar, inexpressive frames. But it can also be a slow, harmonious unfolding, in which your photographs gradually accumulate their own radiance and power to move the viewer—without surrendering to any extreme.

Harmonious development is three times harder than movement toward any extreme. The path of balance is long and cannot yield quick or tangible results. Even in the ideal case, such development remains a gradual drift within the central zone, shifting toward one side of the triangle or another as circumstances require. But if you set as your goal to remain in balance—then it is entirely possible.

By maintaining balance between realism, authorial vision, and intuition, one can transform even daily shots of one's lunch into technically flawless images—subtle, moving, and capable of touching the viewer's heart.

We all begin here, in the valley. Most will never leave it, continuing their daily, half-conscious shooting. Many will set out on the distant path, climbing as far as their strength allows. A few will remain consciously at the center and gradually build the expressiveness and fullness of their photographs.

We all ask ourselves: “Which way should I go?” But the valley's wisdom poses an entirely different question: “To leave or to stay?”

And whatever decision you make, you now have a compass to help you navigate this landscape. It will show you the path to the right destination, but the choice remains yours alone.

And now let us slowly rise up and circle one final time above this metaphorical landscape, taking it in with eyes that see from a bird's-eye height.

It's time to return and look at the language of photography through the prism of these metaphors.

Chapter 7: Triangulation

Chapter 7

Triangulation

Let us begin by examining what real photographs occupy the zones near the peaks of the Photography Triangle.

Beside its objective extreme, you will find photography dedicated to scientific research and cartography, medical imaging and microscopy. Forensic and insurance documentation, the digitization of archives. Technical portraiture for identification and databases, product catalog photography, instructional materials, and much, much more besides.

Survey this vast spectrum of tasks and applications where rigorous objective photography is not merely necessary—it is the only possible approach.

This is why the ability to bring an image “to standard” is the foundational skill you must master before venturing into subjectivity or perception. Paradoxically, by learning to adhere to the rigid technical standards of objective imaging, you simultaneously begin to understand how to transcend those very boundaries. It is precisely the study of bringing images “to standard” that forms the foundation of any basic color correction course—one designed for learning, rather than student entertainment.

Beside photography's subjective extreme, you will find abstract and experimental photography conveying form, color, and movement. Symbolic and metaphorical photography, where objects don't mean what they are. Series of photographs in which the statement is not a single image, but a collection of them. Various versions of performance photography, self-expression through self-portraiture, and the deconstruction of the photographic process itself.

As you may notice, compared to objective photography, the purpose and application of these images is far more narrow—yet their visual diversity is vastly greater.

Such photographs are, above all, an act of authorial statement expressed through photographic language. If they are not parody or mere imitation, they represent the pinnacle of intellectual art in photography.

It's impossible to create a “crash course in art photography for dummies.” Learning to encode complex philosophical concepts in a chaos of lines or just a few spots of light may require not only an art education, knowledge of art history and its current state, but also years of intense creative exploration.

Now, try answering this question yourself: what lies midway between objective and subjective photography? By this point in our discussion, you already know everything you need to find this answer on your own.

Just don't try to place any particular “photographic genre” in this position. Genres have nothing to do with the fundamental interactions that form the basis of photography.

To find your answer, consider this: what parameters of interaction change between objective and subjective photography? There are two.

The first is the author's voice—at its loudest in the subjective extreme. It gradually fades, transforming into complete silence in objective photography.

The second is the subject itself. In objective photography, it simply “is”, preserving its natural essence. But as you move toward the subjective extreme, the subject gradually begins to “mean” something. It transforms into a carrier of aesthetic meaning.

As one more hint, let me remind you: the expressive means of photography—the elements and techniques of composition that form its external appearance—are what shape the photograph. It is precisely their definiteness and unambiguity that create objective photography. It is precisely these—used as a palette—that the author employs to create his own language of communication with the viewer in subjective photography.

Let us begin a gradual movement from objective photography toward the subjective.

An inherent property of photography is linear perspective. Determined by optics and geometry, it carries no authorial message whatsoever. At this point, his voice has not yet been born.

Then, one by one, the first biologically determined compositional techniques appear. They allow the photographer to construct simple messages, much like the first words of a child just learning to speak.

The contrast of warm and cool colors not only communicates “this is cool, and that is warm!” but also separates objects and establishes the emotional mood of the frame.

Atmospheric perspective not only tells “this is near, and that is far!” but also creates a sense of depth and volume.

Gradually, the language of messages becomes more complex and begins to convey small stories. The priority of the left side and the perception of leading lines direct attention: “look there!” and “come here!”—helping to depict movement and the interaction of image elements.

The next level of complexity enables comparing and evaluating objects. Size contrast not only communicates “this is large, it's more important than that small one!” but also allows determining the priority of perception for objects in the frame.

Symmetry emerges, suggesting “this is correct, healthy, and beautiful, while that is uneven—and possibly dangerous: best avoided!”

Next, we gain the ability to speak about what is absent from the frame. The perception of patterns, rhythm, and similarity emerges—telling us “here is safe and calm” or warning us “order is disturbed here: threat!”

At this point, biologically grounded compositional techniques give way to rational ones. These no longer establish hierarchy—they can emerge and combine arbitrarily. Combined with earlier techniques, they enable the expression of complex narratives, much like the sophisticated speech of an adult.

Silhouette establishes the level of abstraction and allows us to imagine what is unseen.

Light and shadow create volume within a flat image and direct our attention.

The viewpoint introduces the observer into the photograph and reveals his position relative to the subject.

The choice of compositional center allows the author to declare what is most important, regardless of its actual size.

Cropping permits telling only the part of the truth one chooses—or, to speak plainly, to lie.

Framing encloses what is essential and emphasizes the relationships within the frame.

Circular, axial, and other movement trajectories allow the photographer to create a visual “journey” for the viewer—a path to follow within the photograph.

Tonal perspective separates spatial planes according to the author's design, not natural gradation.

The choice of focal length permits transforming the perception of both space and subject, establishing different contexts for the scene.

Nuance reveals barely perceptible variations within patterns—addressing consciousness rather than the instinctive comfort of pattern recognition.

One final step remains—and there we stand at the midpoint between objective and subjective photography. Here nature and reason find balance and enhance one another.

The author's language becomes not merely that of an adult, but of a mature human. He does not simply tell his story, but does so in the language of harmony, celebrating the unity of the world and the human being.

This is the final compositional technique—the one that crowns not only photography, but art itself.

Within this point dwells the golden ratio.

First observed in Babylon and Ancient Egypt, it was formulated by Greek thinkers during antiquity. At this same point within the Photography Triangle reside kindred systems of compositional and spatial organization from Eastern traditions: Ma, Feng Shui, and Xiang Shu Xue.

The golden ratio has natural foundations both in the world around us and in the proportions of our bodies. Nevertheless, as an organizing principle, it was extracted from the natural world and formulated by human consciousness itself.

If we compare compositional techniques to human speech, the golden ratio finds its closest parallel in the rhythmic and stylistic structure of verse and hymn. Such organizational systems arose simultaneously in Vedic India, Ancient China, and Ancient Greece between the 15th and 6th centuries BCE—marking a shared cultural moment: the transition from intuition to conscious system-building.

A photograph standing at this point of balance not only commands the widest range of expressive techniques. The harmony between objective and subjective, inscribed within it by the golden ratio, will—regardless of your intention or will—inevitably infuse the image with sacred and philosophical dimensions of perception.

At this point, one hears not the voice of an individual author, but the voice of man as a species that has reached a certain stage of development and has become conscious of its harmony with the surrounding world.

But let us continue our journey toward subjective photography, moving away from the midpoint of the triangle's side. As we travel, the universal “we” of humanity will gradually fragment into distinct groups. The language of universal harmony will begin to divide into local visual languages, becoming comprehensible to ever-narrower audiences.

First emerge the vast cultural clusters of East and West. Then follow religious, regional, national, and group differences in language and imagery. These languages intersect and grant the author freedom to choose: in which visual idiom does he wish to express his thoughts, and with whom he sees himself at this moment. Photography's language permits, within this zone, not only the discourse on the sublime and eternal, but also the narration of daily life—and even the joke.

This fragmentation of humanity ultimately converges at a singular point: the solitary “I” of the author. This is the maximum possible level of freedom of expression—where the author is bound neither by the constraints of group and ethnos, nor by the necessity of rigidly maintaining balance between the human and the natural.

But at the same time, this freedom is also the ultimate level of solitude, where the author's consciousness is locked behind the walls of personal perception.

The only way to overcome these walls is to begin your journey back, moving up along the same edge, in search of those who will understand you.

This is the inevitable path back to harmony.

And the Photography Triangle visibly demonstrates that both the stairway of compositional techniques, leading from matter, and the stairway of photographic languages, leading from the inner world of man, lead to this harmony.

These two stairways are like two dimensions, in which movement occurs independently and in parallel.

The compositional elements that serve as tools for dividing space in objective photography simultaneously participate in forming the language of communication in subjective photography.

The golden ratio is the point where these two stairways converge, forming perfect harmony. But reaching this harmony is possible only if you have climbed to it along both stairways at once—achieving maximum compositional mastery and mastering a universal method of expressing your thoughts.

But have we not overlooked something important? After all, the Photography Triangle has its third, perceptual extreme state. What real images can we see beside it?

We will not see them, just as we do not see on social media posts about confessions or therapy sessions. Moreover, from the perspective of perceptual photography, publishing such images would mislead others from the path of gaining their own visual experience.

Unlike the axis between objective and subjective, where we can distribute elements with sufficient precision and establish their gradation, you will have to mark out the perceptual axis yourself.

Take a sheet of paper and a pencil, and write down what lies personally for you beyond the perceptual vertex. Depending on what's there, mark the gradations on the sides of the triangle that lead to the perceptual vertex from the objective and subjective points. If you doubt the number of these gradations or what they should be, try to find these answers in books, in conversations with representatives of your faith, or with a psychologist. This is the field of your personal work and responsibility. No one else can do it, because the perceptual axis of the Photography Triangle passes through your personal perception of reality.

In this book, I will write the words “the immeasurable” beyond the perceptual vertex.

These three stairways allow you to move independently toward the center of the triangle, to that common point where matter, consciousness, and the immeasurable converge together, forming perfect unity.

Recall the map of the triangle: its center is the world of ordinary people and seers. That place where one can both sink into routine and achieve complete harmony with the world.

But perhaps you are convinced that the sacred does not exist, nor moral law, nor the unconscious. Then simply use only the axis between objective and subjective, because that will be sufficient for creating images. If, however, your view of the world begins to change over time and a new dimension appears in it, the triangle will allow you to reflect these changes in your photographs as well.

The Photography Triangle does not dictate who you should be, what you should or shouldn't believe. Like photography itself, it gives the possibility to find a place for other perspectives in your worldview. And your personal view of reality is an integral part of it.

And so now you know enough about its structure, its organization, and the forces that create it.

I suggest you take a brief pause, after which we will examine several examples of its application in practice.

Chapter 8: Orientation in the Field

Chapter 8

Orientation in the Field

You might think: “This is all so strange... Tales, then philosophy... How am I supposed to use all this?”﻿.

The author has not lost touch with reality or wandered into the clouds. But within the scope of this book, there seems to be no other way of presenting things that does not depend on differences in worldview than the language of parable. As for the concepts we've discussed, we did not invent them, nor did we accept them as dogma. We derived them step by step from the very process of interaction between photographer, viewer, and subject.

To understand how to use the triangle, two principles will help you: goal-setting and the principle of least action.

Goal-setting allows you to define the desired result of your shooting and processing. Only then will you understand which direction to move. As for the principle of least action, it can be expressed simply: “If you can avoid doing something, don't do it. If you cannot avoid it, then do it absolutely.”

For example, the absence of a specific goal is itself a goal. Sometimes you simply need to “shoot, adjust, and deliver,” and you have no additional information to guide you.

In this case, we take the triangle and find a zone where the author's voice is minimal, there is no movement along the perceptual axis, and the subject itself is strongly manifest. Since the task is not clearly defined, the sought position is not the objective vertex, but rather a small elongated zone somewhat removed from it.

You can return to the chapter on objective photography and find the parameters you should aim for when adjusting the image. You don't need to strictly follow them all, since the zone allows some flexibility. You can shift the central subject from the geometric center and not observe the match to original colors as strictly as in the case of an ideal objective photograph. This flexibility allows you to cautiously apply some of the biologically grounded compositional techniques.

Analyze which parameters in your photograph don't match the necessary ones, and correct them. If the task is truly so undefined, it's better to limit yourself to basic correction than to add a style that might turn out to be inappropriate.

Clarifying the goal will change the zone where the image is located within the triangle, which will inevitably influence how you work with the image.

Perhaps you can define the task a bit more specifically—for example, with words like “I want to make it more joyful.”

You can try to express “make it more joyful” as a desire to convey a light, uplifted mood and positive emotion. Let's look at the triangle and find, on the axis between objective and subjective, its upper quarter, where biologically perceived compositional elements are located. We can freely use them to accomplish this task. But if the task were formulated as “The joy of...”, this would imply a conscious action and would allow us to move into the zone of rational compositional elements located closer to the subjective vertex of the triangle.

As for adjusting the captured image, “more joyful” can be conveyed with slightly elevated brightness, contrast, and saturation, along with properly set sharpness. White balance will be shifted toward warmer tones for most subjects, and toward cooler tones for subjects dominated by water, sky, and snow.

Identify which parameters in your image don't match the required ones, and make the necessary adjustments.

If you think the perceptual axis of the Photography Triangle can only be used in creative photography or in inner work on self-transformation, that's not the case.

It—just like the other axes of the triangle—has a gradient that varies from absolute to its minimum.

If its highest point could be described with the word “insight,” then as clarity of perception diminishes you would place along it the words “illumination,” “revelation,” “hunch,” “premonition,” and—near its beginning—“intuition.”

Try using the technique of active perception mentioned in the chapter on perceptual photography when selecting or editing your images. The ability to engage intuition, premonition, or hunch at the right moment can help you perform selection and adjustment more quickly.

In this part of the perceptual axis, you won't reach the heights of perception or grasp the deep beauty within each photograph. Instead, you will simply choose those images that are more successful or more beautiful.

I apologize in advance for the following down‑to‑earth analogy involving smell. Perhaps you've tried picking the ripest melon from a pile in a store. You could slowly examine each one, comparing their color, the dryness of the stem, weight, and spots. But the fastest and most effective way is to do it by smell—a skill that can be developed if you wish.

Treat intuition like smell. If you regularly practice developing your intuitive perception, you lose nothing, even if results don't appear right away.

And if moving along the perceptual axis into the intuition zone will never harm you, I would like to warn you against thoughtless movement to that point of the triangle where the golden ratio is located.

Its application, regardless of your intention, can introduce spiritual and metaphysical dimensions of perception into your images. If your subject is distant from these qualities, it's worth refraining from harmonizing your images using the golden ratio.

First, it can not only appear absurd, but also impose layers of meaning onto your images that you yourself don't want to see. And second, you'll most likely find such images quite boring.

A primary subject positioned at the golden ratio point (≈ 1.618:1) inevitably acquires the serenity of a sage who has found truth and ceased his searching.

Are you certain you want this effect in your images?

For harmonizing most subjects, it is better to use the rule of thirds (1:2) instead. It gained widespread popularity thanks to military photography manuals and training guides published after World War II. Dividing the frame's side into three simple parts required no precise calculations, made teaching easier, and produced acceptable results. Later, an equals sign was mistakenly placed between the rule of thirds and the golden ratio. This confusion became so deeply entrenched that many people still believe they are synonyms. It was ultimately reinforced by the mass introduction of 3/3 compositional grids in cameras.

The rule of thirds is one of the indispensable elements of modern global photographic language. You will encounter its application everywhere—in amateur and professional photographs, in advertising and journalism, in traditional media and social networks.

The reason for its mass popularity is not difficult to understand when viewed through the lens of the Photography Triangle. The rule of thirds, in fact, perfectly reflects the inner state of contemporary man.

He is educated enough to shift composition from center, yet he can't achieve wisdom, preferring simplification. He constantly seeks harmony but chooses dynamism and imbalance instead. He is eternally dissatisfied and internally tense.

The rule of thirds embodies his perpetual search and endless motion toward what lies within arm's reach.

Yet sometimes even composition built on the rule of thirds can appear inappropriately static.

If you're planning to photograph a nightclub party on a Friday evening, you'd better choose something entirely different. For your images to convincingly convey the event's atmosphere to its participants, random application of compositional techniques simply won't suffice.

You should work with the opposite side of the axis between subjective and objective, and devise your own photographic language for this event.

Based on the subject matter, it shouldn't be complicated, but it must be vivid—and possibly even absurd.

Should this language be tailored to just one specific group you're photographing? After all, a language for a “pajama party” would require a thematic approach, while regular work with a club demands something more universal and neutral.

If everyone will be celebrating and cutting loose, consider how to convey this through compositional techniques. Since a party is a day of disobedience, use everything in your shooting that you normally carefully avoid, and in post-processing emphasize what you usually correct.

Apply absurd cropping, don't fear overexposure and excessive contrast. Emphasize unrelated objects with absurd compositional lines. Increase saturation and push colors toward the “strange” and “unusual.”

The grotesque and paradoxical photographic language you ultimately create will be a true language of harmony.

If approaching the golden ratio from the objective vertex, we strive for harmony in the elements of a composition, then approaching it from the subjective vertex, we strive for harmony of form and message content. I hope now you understand why the golden ratio is so demanding of the subjects it can be applied to.

But the Photography Triangle can serve not only as a compass helping you find the right path during shooting and post-processing. You can also use it to navigate your creative journey.

Try marking on it not individual images, but your series or large photo projects.

See whether they cluster together or remain scattered far apart.

Can you perceive them as a unified zone? Or do you feel they are fragmented with nothing connecting them?

If it's a zone—do you want to compress it, expand it, or shift it in some direction?

If they're separate points—do you feel the urge to unite them or cut away the excess?

Or do they form a pattern, and you feel like continuing its drawing across the field of photography?

You can go even further and try to find a place in the triangle not for your photographs, but for yourself as a photographer.

Is your current position in it a zone or a point? Are you comfortable seeing it in this place in the triangle, or do you feel the urge to move it somewhere else? Do you want to compress it to concentrate your efforts, or expand its size, exploring something new?

Reflect on your answers and remember them. You can repeat this analysis from time to time, even if you're not shooting anything fundamentally different. Over time, your perspective will gradually shift, which will lead to a reassessment of your position in the photographic field. You'll be able to track whether your position remains static or moves, whether it circles or sharply changes its direction.

The Photography Triangle allows you to find your place in photography not in a grandiose metaphorical sense, but in a practical one—marked with a pencil on a sheet of paper. And just as practically, it allows you to trace your path through it, as a trajectory connecting waypoints. The only thing you need to do is be honest with yourself.

The Photography Triangle is a sufficiently universal tool for analyzing your images, photo projects, and creative work as a whole. It can take its rightful place in your photographic arsenal alongside the tripod, lens hood, and light meter. But just as with any other photography tool, it will be entirely up to you whether you remain its owner, become its user, or apply it to create new photographs.

This is your choice to make.

And I would probably end my story here, if not for one more phenomenon worth examining through the lens of the Photography Triangle.

Chapter 9: The New Reality

Chapter 9

The New Reality

You cannot ignore this, even if you wanted to. The emergence and rapid evolution of neural networks have already affected the lives of hundreds of millions of people, one way or another, and this is only the beginning.

The images they generate pose an acute question before us—what is photography, really? Where are its boundaries? Should we reinforce them or completely reconsider them?

Today, AI-generated images are visually indistinguishable from photographs for most viewers, and even specialists need time to determine whether they're looking at a real photo or a generated image. They don't simply compete with photography—they are actively displacing it thanks to instant creation, low cost, and the absence of skill requirements beyond formulating a text prompt.

This book is devoted to photography, the interactions that create it, and a tool for analyzing them. But these interactions, as you may have already noticed, are universal and, like infinite axes, extend far beyond photography as a form of visual art.

Let's make use of this and examine AI-generated images from each vertex of the Photography Triangle.

First, let's recall what lies behind objective photography and what constitutes its very essence.

Of the three elements of photographic interaction, the photographer and camera are maximally weakened in it, which brings the viewer's perception of the subject to the foreground.

What will we see when looking at AI-generated images from the perspective of objective photography?

The place of the photographer has been taken by the prompter—a person formulating text requests, and the place of the camera has been taken by the generative model being used.

Both the photographer and the prompter create images, but what is the difference between them?

To describe the work of a photographer, the Latin word artifex would be most accurate—a master and artist who creates art through their mind and hands and possesses technical skill.

And to describe what the prompter does, the Latin word consultans—one who seeks counsel from an oracle for a “vision”—would be far more fitting. And if the supplication to the neuro-oracle is formulated correctly, they will receive that “vision” in the form of a generated image.

This is a very revealing detail, but one that barely influences anything within objective photography. Don't forget that the prompter's influence, just like the photographer's influence in objective photography, is minimal.

The viewer's role has not changed at all, and they may not even suspect the most crucial detail. The objects they perceive in the AI-generated image, formed by the combination of brightness, contrast, and compositional elements—do not exist in reality.

Photography served as an imperfect yet necessary vessel, conveying to the viewer the precious contents of reality.

AI-generated images, by contrast, are an empty vessel, delivering not a single drop of it to them.

From the vertex of objective photography, you can clearly see legalized piracy in image‑generation platforms. The datasets used to train neural networks were unlawfully obtained by copying images from all available sources without the permission of the rights holders. Numerous lawsuits filed against developer companies by classical media outlets, photo banks, and private photographers are typically resolved through settlement or dismissed by courts based on legal technicalities. Not a single developer company has attempted to contest the accusations on their merits, building no defense based on evidence of the legality of the datasets they used to build their business.

Generations of photographers, curators, and specialists created world photography as a form of visual art. Generations of viewers evaluated it, participating in the photographic process and selecting the best works.

Photographers or media outlets whose images were used without their knowledge can at least attempt to contest their rights in court. Millions and millions of viewers whose attention was stolen along with the images lack such an opportunity even theoretically. This very “concentrate of meaning,” formed by photography and viewer attention, is the true object of appropriation by developer companies. It is this concentrated human visual experience that has allowed generative models to develop so rapidly.

Modern AI images are essentially a cocktail mixed on the basis of real photographs. The next generations of image generators will inevitably, to a greater degree, be trained on already-generated content due to its visual indistinguishability from photographs.

The consequence of this is the inevitable degradation of that universal human visual experience, the appropriation and use of which allows AI giants to reap their fleeting profit today.

No technological innovation in history has been so destructive to human visual culture. All new technologies in the past allowed for the creation of images faster, but that speed was limited by the simple fact that humans applied them. Today, endless streams of visual spam are generated by bots, not people. When painting was displaced by photography, the speed of image production increased from pedestrian pace to automobile speed. The speed at which bot factories generate images today is the speed of a jet missile.

From the perspective of objective photography, an image is a two-dimensional representation of an object or scene that existed in physical reality.

One generative image, almost indistinguishable to the viewer from a photograph—is a lie. Their avalanche-like multiplying collective, before our very eyes replacing the captured reality—is cancer, already afflicting visual culture.

What this will ultimately lead to, we will be able to see for ourselves very soon.

This perspective is bound to give one pause. But in order to try to balance it, let's attempt to view what is happening from the perspective of subjective photography. Perhaps from there, the situation will look less grim?

When viewed from the subjective vertex, we first and foremost see the prompter, into which the author has transformed. His goal is the same—to transcend the boundaries of his consciousness and find contact with the viewer through the images he creates.

He still represents an “I,” conscious of the world and itself through its inner monologue. The judgments, evaluations, and self-descriptions of this “I” can be accomplished only through one of the natural languages in which this inner monologue takes place.

It is precisely in natural language that the prompter formulates his request for image generation. His thought will nonetheless be transformed into combinations of brightness, contrast, and compositional elements, but now not through the photographic process, but through the generative model.

As for the complete absence of a subject to photograph—from the subjective vertex, it was always merely one of the means of expressing the author's creative intent. The absence of this physically existing object allows for an additional degree of freedom—no longer is it necessary to search for a model or subject, to select costumes or wait for the right weather, to adjust lighting or choose a camera angle.

But if you think that from the perspective of subjective photography, the liberation from a subject to photograph or the use of natural language makes generative images attractive—then you would be greatly mistaken.

Subjective photography aims at forming solidarity between author and viewers. This exchange of images helps separate “I”s, through the commonality of experience, to feel themselves as a single “we.” It is precisely this humanistic goal that makes the visual language itself—the very language in which such communication becomes possible—one of the principal values from the perspective of subjective photography.

Any actions leading to the degradation of visual language can be perceived from the subjective vertex of the triangle exclusively as a step toward the destruction of human solidarity and visual segregation.

The visual language of an image is not the individual compositional elements; it is the meaning they acquire in a particular cultural context. And it is precisely these culturally conditioned meanings that serve as parts of speech from which the author can construct a phrase of his visual message.

When compiling the datasets on which generative networks were trained, both the cultural context and the meanings of various elements within it were nearly completely lost. Yes, thanks to vast arrays of images, various combinations of colors, objects, or styles will purely statistically coincide with the theme of the text prompt. But this is an extremely vulgarized and generalized vocabulary of the language you need.

The very visual message itself, which uses these elements as parts of speech, is neither analyzed by the neural network during training nor created by it during generation.

A generative image is a collection of words related to a particular theme and not forming any meaningful message.

You can easily verify this with the example of natural, not visual, language. Try asking any neural network to generate a large image of a hand pointing to a line of clearly readable text in a book. Specify the historical and cultural context that makes sense to you, but give no instructions regarding the text itself. Look at the mangled words mixed with illegible symbols and containing grammatical errors. Even if individual words can be read and even somehow relate to the specified period—their totality will carry no meaning whatsoever.

The same thing happens with visual language. Caught in the meat grinder of generative intelligence, fragments of other people's photographs are squeezed out to you in the form of multicolored, but quite homogeneous mincemeat.

Any person, even imitating other people's creativity and not understanding the details of what is being copied, will nonetheless convey some part of it, even if distorted. Moreover, what is being copied will inevitably pass through the prism of their personal cultural context, enriching the copy with new layers of meaning. Generative networks, on the other hand, do not copy; they imitate the presence of meaning.

Yes, an author can, through repeated refinements and the composition of a detailed prompt, attempt to bring the generation result under his control. Moreover, we could even reason that the composition of a generation request can be compared to the creation of a graphic language of an image and will contain both its own “compositional techniques” and the creation of the necessary “composition of the request” from them.

But such an authorial approach to image generation will in no way be able to influence what is happening as a whole.

The vast majority of generative content is created not by people, but by bots, and the request for generation to the graphic imitator is composed by a linguistic imitator. The external similarity of these images to photographs and the avalanche-like speed of their spread already now lead to the inevitable—the degradation of visual languages.

Chaotically mixed elements, no longer carrying meaning, gradually train the viewer to abandon the perception of anything in images except their external form.

The developers of generative models are private companies, whose primary goal is to generate income for their shareholders. Everything they do will be subordinate to this goal.

In order to increase profits, they need to make their imperfect products accepted and used by the majority of users, which can be achieved in various ways.

To implement the analysis of photographic message in dataset images, as well as to account for it when generating images from a request, is hardly a technically feasible task. And even if it were possible, it would require giant investments in development that would not allow for quick profits.

But there is another way to make the already existing developments more acceptable to the majority—not to prevent their application, which leads to the degradation of overall visual culture. Against such a background, their developments will look quite acceptable and be applied everywhere.

This path requires no investment, is legally safe, and given the current speed of “noise‑flooding” of visual culture with spam content—is quite rapid.

There is no malice in this, just that business efficiency is always the ratio of invested costs to achieved results.

But from the perspective of humanity as a whole, both the actions and inactions of companies aimed at destroying visual language lead to one thing—the deliberate division of mankind.

This is a moral crime and a violation of the foundations of ethics. This is an attempt to destroy universal human solidarity. This is a communicative tragedy leading to the loss of mutual understanding.

And this is that rare case when the voices speaking from both the objective and subjective peaks of the triangle converge in their conclusions.

These voices are heard perfectly well down there, in the middle-earth. But the only thing they evoke here is a slight condescending smile, the kind adults smile at children discussing Santa Claus.

It is here that both those who created these developments and those who use them are found.

Here it is no secret to anyone that at the foundation of this technology lies images obtained dishonestly. But everyone uses them, knowing that the police will not come for you personally.

Yes, the owner of a bot factory generating streams of visual spam is, at an accelerated pace, helping to build a fragmented world that has lost part of its culture and its sense of reality—a world in which his children will have to live tomorrow. But today he will feed them ice cream and take them to the water park.

Probably, an editor, instead of purchasing an image, generating a colored “plug” for an advertising message, is not allowing photography to develop, and this will lead to its rapid stagnation. But he did it instantly, and the “lifespan” of this message will not exceed a couple of days anyway.

Yes, most likely the image of a generative artist is meaningless, but who and how will prove it? After all, some meaning will be perceived even in a random set of words. Perhaps that is what he meant…

Middle-earth is enormous. This is not a place where principles matter. Here everyone understands everything. This is precisely what allows image generation technology to develop successfully and at an accelerated pace and quickly displace everything else.

This technology — undoubtedly has a successful and promising future.

But if we begin moving toward the perceptual peak, all this noise of the battle between idealism and pragmatism begins to subside, gradually turning into silence.

If we are all already in the turbulent stream, what is the point of shouting at it or pounding the water with our fists?

Yes, it is easiest to relax and splash around merrily in it, paying no attention to the approaching roar of the waterfall. Yes, one can try rowing against the current for as long as strength lasts, hoping to return back to the source.

But instead, one can try to swim across to the other shore and continue the journey, leaving the stream behind.

And to find the right direction to that shore, let us look at generative images from the perceptual peak of the triangle.

The external viewer in perceptual practice has no significance, and the true viewer becomes not the photographer, but the prompter using a generative model to create images. But how should we relate to the subject of the shot that is explicitly absent?

Will this absence be a reason for you to completely refuse working with generative images?

This will depend primarily on what you personally have located beyond the perceptual peak.

If you are a nonbeliever—you will most likely consider any image, regardless of its origin, merely a stimulus provoking a reaction in your psyche. Does it really matter to you whether this stimulus was captured by a camera or created by an algorithm?

If you are a believer—will you consider the place of the absent object filled with divine presence? Or conversely, will you perceive it as a violation of the order of things and a perversion of the supreme design?

If you are a Buddhist—then since the essence of any form is emptiness, the absence of this form will not have substantial significance.

I will suggest that for the majority of those who practice the perceptual path, the very absence of the photographic object will not in itself be a reason to refuse using generative images.

But unfortunately, AI-generated images have other characteristics that complicate their use in perceptual work. Let us mark out the most fundamental of them.

First, generated images are statistical patterns obtained through the analysis of arrays of photographs and modulated by your request. One could call the object in generative images the visual patterns extracted from the works of other authors and combined into a given form.

All those photographs that underlie your generative image have already passed through the perception process of the photographers who captured them.

Look at such an image as a stained-glass window made up of small shards of different glass. Some of them are transparent, some almost black, some colored, and almost all of them are somehow distorted. Yes, some light can pass through such a window, but seeing what lies behind it will be practically impossible.

Second, the generative model forms an image based precisely on a text prompt, which is incompatible with the cessation of internal monologue. Any changes that will need to be made will also require you to verbalize them.

How, in this case, should you technically organize active observation? Entering this state is not easy, and to use a generative model you must enter and exit it repeatedly and almost instantaneously?

Third, most generative models are trained in advance and do not make changes to their work as a result of executing requests. They do not learn anything new, there is no accumulation of meaning in the process of interaction with users, they cannot have insights or revelations.

The results you get are like an endless rotation of a kaleidoscope. Each time the result will differ somewhat from the previous one, but completely new colors or forms will never appear.

Fourth, generative models do not even provide the randomness of a kaleidoscope.

To generate an image, they select the pattern that best matches the text of your request among all photographs on which they were trained. What you get without clarifying your request is an averaged image that resembles the most banal of already existing shots on the topic of your request.

If you ask to create “something more original,” they simply select a rarer pattern from those already available. As a result, you get a slightly less banal image, but still similar to what has already been shot.

Even the listed reasons may be sufficient to completely refuse using generative models and once again take a camera in your hands.

Unfortunately, the current implementation of this technology is designed not for you, but for the quick satisfaction of requests from an average and not very demanding user.

But, strangely enough, it is precisely this thought that compels one not to abandon attempts and to continue searching for ways to use AI images in perceptual practice.

The fact is that the same can be said about the camera itself.

Decades of camera development were aimed at achieving the same goal—obtaining a “correct” picture without the need to understand photographic principles. The democratization of photography in the early 20th century took place under Kodak's slogan “You press the button, we do the rest.” The gradually appearing automatic exposure, automatic aperture, TTL light meter, program mode, autofocus in cameras served precisely this goal.

The sum of these developments led to the emergence and mass distribution of the “point and shoot” concept. The emergence of digital cameras led to the possibility of instantly obtaining an image. Computational photography in modern smartphones led to the automation of even image processing, as well as the elimination of the need to “capture the moment” through pre-capture technology. The emergence of generative models is merely another logical step in the same direction, finally allowing one to abandon the camera itself.

With each successive step of automation, users willingly exchanged part of their creative freedom for the convenience of using the technology. But the price paid for this convenience was a gradual narrowing of the ability to influence the resulting image with each new automatic function.

The current implementation of generative models completely deprives the user of the ability to make changes to the resulting image within the model. He can either accept the generation result as it is, or repeat it anew. Complete convenience naturally demands complete abandonment of creative freedom.

But not everyone was willing to give up this freedom. In parallel with the automation of photography, photographers engaged in creative photography also gradually learned to bypass the camera's intrusive assistance or use its algorithms unconventionally to achieve their desired results.

Depending on the goals before them, this could be either a complete rejection of automatic functions, or the use of randomness to destroy predictability in lomography, or the use of non-standard optics or pinhole cameras, or shooting with long exposures, or creating their own cameras or complete rejection of cameras when creating photograms.

All of these, as well as many other ways to influence the photographic process or camera operation, served one common goal—to regain control over image creation and to decide oneself how it should look and what parameters it should correspond to.

But perceptual practice allows one to look at the problem more broadly.

Seeing in a photographer with a camera or prompter with a generative model an inseparable pair, it does not blame specific technologies for everything, but points to the prerequisites for creating stereotypical images according to established patterns in the consciousness of the photographer or prompter.

Regaining control over image creation from a perceptual perspective is necessary not as an end in itself, but as a means to overcome stereotypical perception of reality, which in most cases differs little from the fully automatic mode of camera operation.

And if the ways to circumvent camera algorithms in perceptual practice have already been developed, then the obstacles to using generative models in it seem insurmountable at the moment.

Nevertheless, I have not abandoned hope that a way to overcome these obstacles might exist.

I want to believe that in time we will be able to find the vulnerable points, “cracks in the program,” as Vilém Flusser described them, through which we can break through into the space of freedom and make generative models, if not our partners in perceptual practice, then at least its convenient tools.

If we continue these attempts, then sooner or later a solution will be found.

But for now—use a camera to achieve your goals. It has been and remains both a faithful instrument in the hands of a photographer capturing reality, and a megaphone amplifying the voice of the author, and a reliable support for one walking the difficult path of inner transformation.

Afterword

Afterword

I had been working as a retoucher on this project for some time already. Nothing unusual or exciting—just technical retouching of a few images created in 3D.

This time it was several night interior shots of a new car model, designed to demonstrate to future buyers the illumination of the dashboard and cabin. These were not the kinds of images you proudly place in your portfolio. The difficulties that arose during their retouching were completely invisible to the viewer, and the visual style applied was not developed by you.

Such images, while necessary, go unnoticed. You can find them when choosing a car's trim configuration on a website or on the tenth page of a catalog, next to a description of its options.

Everything was already ready, except for one thing: black holes still gaped in the car's windows. For several days, the client could not decide what landscape should be visible outside the window.

And so, when the project deadline left no more time for further deliberation, he finally sent a link to a file with a demand to integrate it into the images as quickly as possible.

The file was called “Night Beach with Hills.” I opened it, and winced.

The limited budget of such projects does not allow for shooting a background image “to order”—it is usually simply purchased from one of the stock photo banks. But apparently, this time there was not even time for a search in stock photo banks.

I was sent an AI-generated panorama.

It was a typical result of a generative model's work in all its glory.

In reduced form, the image looked decent. But everything changed as soon as you zoomed in and examined the details.

A small strip of illegible symbols in the place where stock photo banks usually place copyright notices immediately and clearly pointed to the source of the photographs on which the generative model had been trained.

The appearance of the sedimentary rocks that made up the coastal cliffs resembled more of a layered pie and made you want to hold your head in your hands. The same thing was desired when looking at plants of the “Bush averaging incredibilis” variety that covered these cliffs. The horizon line at the edges of the frame split in two and bent downward.

Completely misplaced stones protruded here and there, and right in the center of the panorama stood a huge puddle, which for some reason did not seep into the dry sand.

Suddenly, a familiar soundless click resonated in my head.

I froze, trying to understand what had happened, and afraid to startle this familiar sensation.

Then I habitually rolled my neck, took a few deep breaths, and began to perceive the image as a whole.

This place was not a beach. There was no sea here, no parking lot, no road. It was a steppe at night, or a desert, or the bottom of a dried-up sea.

The silhouettes of a ridge of hills in the background resembled pyramids buried centuries ago. Above them stretched a strange, completely starless, yet flooded with faint luminescence sky.

And down below, at the very center of this desiccated landscape, lay a vast puddle, full of reflections of bright multicolored stars: glowing from either another time or another space.

This image was powerful, majestic, and absolutely soundless. It triggered a chain reaction of associations and meanings emerging through it.

But where did it come from? Who created it? From the perceptual perspective, each person must answer this question for themselves, based on what lies beyond the perceptual apex of their triangle.

It was not created by the person who sent me the file. I contacted him and found out that he simply gave a prompt: “a view of a night beach with hills.” Even when I drew his attention to the reflection, the sky, the pyramids, to the desert instead of a beach—he still saw them only as artifacts.

The generative model did not create this image either. It could not theoretically have gone beyond the given prompt and did everything it could to bring the result closer to it.

Nor was I its author to the same extent—just as I am not the author of a landscape created by frost patterns on glass, or of a white camel floating as a cloud above my head.

In perceptual practice, the question of authorship dissolves like smoke, allowing one to see what emerges beyond it.

I examined the landscape once more, carefully, and tried my best to fix it in my memory.

Then I deleted the puddle with the reflections of stars in it. I removed the second horizon line and the extraneous stones and bushes. I rounded the pyramids, making them resemble hills. I added a starry sky and inserted the resulting image into the file with the car.

I saw no point in claiming it as my own.

What I received was far more valuable.

For a moment, light flashed before me, marking that very crack leading into a space of freedom.

It turns out, the answer had been in plain sight all along, and to find it, one simply needed to remember the elements that form the foundation of the triangle.

After all, both the photographer with a camera and the prompter with a generative model form a dual system in which roles can shift depending on circumstances and move from one element to another.

If unlocking the creative potential of the generative model itself is not yet possible, we can instead try to unlock it through proper interaction with the second element of the system—the prompter.

And that prompter should not be you.

For now, it is merely a conjecture, and I have no exact answers as to what such interaction would look like. But I sense that this could become the right direction in the further search for ways to use AI in perceptual practice.

Who knows—perhaps we will still be able to one day, peering into the murky puddle of generative images, see there an unfamiliar starry sky.

And exclaim, like the character in “2001: A Space Odyssey” gazing at the Monolith: “My God, It's Full of Stars!”

Introduction:

The starting point for developing the Photography Triangle was the analysis of ideas and concepts of the following photographers and photography theorists:

Ansel Adams (1902-1984)

Profession: Landscape photographer, educator

Key concept: Zone System

Specialization: Nature, architecture, technical aspects of photography

Limitations of the concept: Technical aspects only—light, tone, development

Position: Does not address questions of meaning, perception, authorship

Minor White (1908-1976)

Profession: Photographer, educator, editor of Aperture magazine

Key concept: Equivalence

Specialization: Abstract and spiritual photography

Limitations of the concept: Emotional aspects only

Position: Mystical approach without a structured analytical model

Henri Cartier-Bresson (1908-2004)

Profession: Photojournalist, founder of Magnum agency

Key concept: Decisive Moment

Specialization: Street photography, photojournalism

Limitations of the concept: Narrow concept for street photography only

Position: Focused on the moment of capture, does not address processing and philosophical aspects

Edward Weston (1886-1958)

Profession: Photographer, one of the founders of Group f/64

Key concept: Straight Photography

Specialization: Still lifes, landscapes, portraits

Limitations of the concept: Aesthetic doctrine, not an analytical system

Position: Dogmatic approach—does not provide tools for analyzing different approaches

Roland Barthes (1915-1980)

Profession: Philologist, semiotician, critic

Education: Literature, linguistics

Specialization: Professor of literary semiology at the Collège de France

Point of view: Semiotic—studied photography as a system of signs and myths of bourgeois culture

Position: Considered himself an “amateur of photography,” not a photographer

John Berger (1926-2017)

Profession: Art critic, writer, artist

Education: Studied painting at Chelsea School of Art

Specialization: Started as an artist, later became a critic

Point of view: Socio-political—analyzed “ways of seeing” as instruments of power

Position: Studied visual culture from the perspective of class struggle

Susan Sontag (1933-2004)

Profession: Writer, cultural critic, essayist

Education: Philosophy, literature

Specialization: Taught philosophy at Columbia University and Sarah Lawrence College

Point of view: Cultural-critical—analyzed photography as a social phenomenon and instrument of power

Position: Intellectual studying visual culture from the outside

Walter Benjamin (1892-1940)

Profession: Philosopher, literary critic, cultural theorist

Education: Philosophy, German studies

Specialization: Critical theory

Point of view: Studied photography in the context of “technical reproducibility” and the loss of “aura”

Position: Critic of bourgeois culture, theorist of the avant-garde

André Bazin (1918-1958)

Profession: Film critic, film theorist

Education: Philology

Specialization: Founder of Cahiers du cinéma, ontology of cinema and photography

Point of view: Ontological—studied photography as a mechanical recording possessing an intrinsic indexical relation to reality

Position: Viewed photography as the foundation of cinema, not as an independent art form

Vilém Flusser (1920-1991)

Profession: Philosopher, media and communication theorist

Education: Philosophy of language

Specialization: Communication theory, media philosophy
