Chapter 7: Triangulation

Chapter 7

Triangulation

Let us begin by examining what real photographs occupy the zones near the peaks of the Photography Triangle.

Beside its objective extreme, you will find photography dedicated to scientific research and cartography, medical imaging and microscopy. Forensic and insurance documentation, the digitization of archives. Technical portraiture for identification and databases, product catalog photography, instructional materials, and much, much more besides.

Survey this vast spectrum of tasks and applications where rigorous objective photography is not merely necessary—it is the only possible approach.

This is why the ability to bring an image “to standard” is the foundational skill you must master before venturing into subjectivity or perception. Paradoxically, by learning to adhere to the rigid technical standards of objective imaging, you simultaneously begin to understand how to transcend those very boundaries. It is precisely the study of bringing images “to standard” that forms the foundation of any basic color correction course—one designed for learning, rather than student entertainment.

Beside photography's subjective extreme, you will find abstract and experimental photography conveying form, color, and movement. Symbolic and metaphorical photography, where objects don't mean what they are. Series of photographs in which the statement is not a single image, but a collection of them. Various versions of performance photography, self-expression through self-portraiture, and the deconstruction of the photographic process itself.

As you may notice, compared to objective photography, the purpose and application of these images is far more narrow—yet their visual diversity is vastly greater.

Such photographs are, above all, an act of authorial statement expressed through photographic language. If they are not parody or mere imitation, they represent the pinnacle of intellectual art in photography.

It's impossible to create a “crash course in art photography for dummies.” Learning to encode complex philosophical concepts in a chaos of lines or just a few spots of light may require not only an art education, knowledge of art history and its current state, but also years of intense creative exploration.

Now, try answering this question yourself: what lies midway between objective and subjective photography? By this point in our discussion, you already know everything you need to find this answer on your own.

Just don't try to place any particular “photographic genre” in this position. Genres have nothing to do with the fundamental interactions that form the basis of photography.

To find your answer, consider this: what parameters of interaction change between objective and subjective photography? There are two.

The first is the author's voice—at its loudest in the subjective extreme. It gradually fades, transforming into complete silence in objective photography.

The second is the subject itself. In objective photography, it simply “is”, preserving its natural essence. But as you move toward the subjective extreme, the subject gradually begins to “mean” something. It transforms into a carrier of aesthetic meaning.

As one more hint, let me remind you: the expressive means of photography—the elements and techniques of composition that form its external appearance—are what shape the photograph. It is precisely their definiteness and unambiguity that create objective photography. It is precisely these—used as a palette—that the author employs to create his own language of communication with the viewer in subjective photography.

Let us begin a gradual movement from objective photography toward the subjective.

An inherent property of photography is linear perspective. Determined by optics and geometry, it carries no authorial message whatsoever. At this point, his voice has not yet been born.

Then, one by one, the first biologically determined compositional techniques appear. They allow the photographer to construct simple messages, much like the first words of a child just learning to speak.

The contrast of warm and cool colors not only communicates “this is cool, and that is warm!” but also separates objects and establishes the emotional mood of the frame.

Atmospheric perspective not only tells “this is near, and that is far!” but also creates a sense of depth and volume.

Gradually, the language of messages becomes more complex and begins to convey small stories. The priority of the left side and the perception of leading lines direct attention: “look there!” and “come here!”—helping to depict movement and the interaction of image elements.

The next level of complexity enables comparing and evaluating objects. Size contrast not only communicates “this is large, it's more important than that small one!” but also allows determining the priority of perception for objects in the frame.

Symmetry emerges, suggesting “this is correct, healthy, and beautiful, while that is uneven—and possibly dangerous: best avoided!”

Next, we gain the ability to speak about what is absent from the frame. The perception of patterns, rhythm, and similarity emerges—telling us “here is safe and calm” or warning us “order is disturbed here: threat!”

At this point, biologically grounded compositional techniques give way to rational ones. These no longer establish hierarchy—they can emerge and combine arbitrarily. Combined with earlier techniques, they enable the expression of complex narratives, much like the sophisticated speech of an adult.

Silhouette establishes the level of abstraction and allows us to imagine what is unseen.

Light and shadow create volume within a flat image and direct our attention.

The viewpoint introduces the observer into the photograph and reveals his position relative to the subject.

The choice of compositional center allows the author to declare what is most important, regardless of its actual size.

Cropping permits telling only the part of the truth one chooses—or, to speak plainly, to lie.

Framing encloses what is essential and emphasizes the relationships within the frame.

Circular, axial, and other movement trajectories allow the photographer to create a visual “journey” for the viewer—a path to follow within the photograph.

Tonal perspective separates spatial planes according to the author's design, not natural gradation.

The choice of focal length permits transforming the perception of both space and subject, establishing different contexts for the scene.

Nuance reveals barely perceptible variations within patterns—addressing consciousness rather than the instinctive comfort of pattern recognition.

One final step remains—and there we stand at the midpoint between objective and subjective photography. Here nature and reason find balance and enhance one another.

The author's language becomes not merely that of an adult, but of a mature human. He does not simply tell his story, but does so in the language of harmony, celebrating the unity of the world and the human being.

This is the final compositional technique—the one that crowns not only photography, but art itself.

Within this point dwells the golden ratio.

First observed in Babylon and Ancient Egypt, it was formulated by Greek thinkers during antiquity. At this same point within the Photography Triangle reside kindred systems of compositional and spatial organization from Eastern traditions: Ma, Feng Shui, and Xiang Shu Xue.

The golden ratio has natural foundations both in the world around us and in the proportions of our bodies. Nevertheless, as an organizing principle, it was extracted from the natural world and formulated by human consciousness itself.

If we compare compositional techniques to human speech, the golden ratio finds its closest parallel in the rhythmic and stylistic structure of verse and hymn. Such organizational systems arose simultaneously in Vedic India, Ancient China, and Ancient Greece between the 15th and 6th centuries BCE—marking a shared cultural moment: the transition from intuition to conscious system-building.

A photograph standing at this point of balance not only commands the widest range of expressive techniques. The harmony between objective and subjective, inscribed within it by the golden ratio, will—regardless of your intention or will—inevitably infuse the image with sacred and philosophical dimensions of perception.

At this point, one hears not the voice of an individual author, but the voice of man as a species that has reached a certain stage of development and has become conscious of its harmony with the surrounding world.

But let us continue our journey toward subjective photography, moving away from the midpoint of the triangle's side. As we travel, the universal “we” of humanity will gradually fragment into distinct groups. The language of universal harmony will begin to divide into local visual languages, becoming comprehensible to ever-narrower audiences.

First emerge the vast cultural clusters of East and West. Then follow religious, regional, national, and group differences in language and imagery. These languages intersect and grant the author freedom to choose: in which visual idiom does he wish to express his thoughts, and with whom he sees himself at this moment. Photography's language permits, within this zone, not only the discourse on the sublime and eternal, but also the narration of daily life—and even the joke.

This fragmentation of humanity ultimately converges at a singular point: the solitary “I” of the author. This is the maximum possible level of freedom of expression—where the author is bound neither by the constraints of group and ethnos, nor by the necessity of rigidly maintaining balance between the human and the natural.

But at the same time, this freedom is also the ultimate level of solitude, where the author's consciousness is locked behind the walls of personal perception.

The only way to overcome these walls is to begin your journey back, moving up along the same edge, in search of those who will understand you.

This is the inevitable path back to harmony.

And the Photography Triangle visibly demonstrates that both the stairway of compositional techniques, leading from matter, and the stairway of photographic languages, leading from the inner world of man, lead to this harmony.

These two stairways are like two dimensions, in which movement occurs independently and in parallel.

The compositional elements that serve as tools for dividing space in objective photography simultaneously participate in forming the language of communication in subjective photography.

The golden ratio is the point where these two stairways converge, forming perfect harmony. But reaching this harmony is possible only if you have climbed to it along both stairways at once—achieving maximum compositional mastery and mastering a universal method of expressing your thoughts.

But have we not overlooked something important? After all, the Photography Triangle has its third, perceptual extreme state. What real images can we see beside it?

We will not see them, just as we do not see on social media posts about confessions or therapy sessions. Moreover, from the perspective of perceptual photography, publishing such images would mislead others from the path of gaining their own visual experience.

Unlike the axis between objective and subjective, where we can distribute elements with sufficient precision and establish their gradation, you will have to mark out the perceptual axis yourself.

Take a sheet of paper and a pencil, and write down what lies personally for you beyond the perceptual vertex. Depending on what's there, mark the gradations on the sides of the triangle that lead to the perceptual vertex from the objective and subjective points. If you doubt the number of these gradations or what they should be, try to find these answers in books, in conversations with representatives of your faith, or with a psychologist. This is the field of your personal work and responsibility. No one else can do it, because the perceptual axis of the Photography Triangle passes through your personal perception of reality.

In this book, I will write the words “the immeasurable” beyond the perceptual vertex.

These three stairways allow you to move independently toward the center of the triangle, to that common point where matter, consciousness, and the immeasurable converge together, forming perfect unity.

Recall the map of the triangle: its center is the world of ordinary people and seers. That place where one can both sink into routine and achieve complete harmony with the world.

But perhaps you are convinced that the sacred does not exist, nor moral law, nor the unconscious. Then simply use only the axis between objective and subjective, because that will be sufficient for creating images. If, however, your view of the world begins to change over time and a new dimension appears in it, the triangle will allow you to reflect these changes in your photographs as well.

The Photography Triangle does not dictate who you should be, what you should or shouldn't believe. Like photography itself, it gives the possibility to find a place for other perspectives in your worldview. And your personal view of reality is an integral part of it.

And so now you know enough about its structure, its organization, and the forces that create it.

I suggest you take a brief pause, after which we will examine several examples of its application in practice.
