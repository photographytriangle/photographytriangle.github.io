What This Book Is About and How It Can Help You

My name is Alexander Zabara. I’m a photographer, color grader, and educator. I’ve been working with retouching and color correction for almost 30 years, and I’ve been teaching image processing for about 15 years.

In developing my image processing course, I was faced with an essential need: to create a universal framework—a model to guide the processing of any photograph.

My challenge was to teach photographers of vastly different backgrounds and styles. The framework I needed had to be genuinely universal—independent of genre, of the photographer’s personality, and of their worldview. I needed a system that encompassed all of photography: one that honored both its technical demands and its creative potential.

Hoping to find the answer in established research, I turned to the classical texts on photographic theory.

But, to my great surprise, I discovered that no such unified system existed.

None of the systems created by photographic theorists were universal. The authors of these works focused on specific technical, genre-based, or ideological aspects, and their theories didn’t encompass photography as a whole.

The authors of books on the philosophy of photography, for the most part, were literary scholars or critics, not photographers. They saw photography primarily as a cultural phenomenon, and their theories were not intended for practical application by photographers.

And none of these theories could be directly applied to the process of color correction.

Based on this research, I began to independently search for patterns in photography that could serve as the foundation for such a universal system of description. The imperfection of existing theories, despite their variety, suggested that choosing any particular basic elements of photography as the foundation would not allow for describing all the diversity that photography can represent.

This led me to the idea that the foundation of such a universal system should not be individual basic elements of photography, but rather the process of interaction between them.

Both individual elements of photographic interaction and some of the connections between them had already been found by different authors. But the unified system they form, and the common logic of these connections, apparently had not been previously recognized.

Built on the basis of these interactions, the system I named the Photography Triangle, even in its basic form, worked quite well in accomplishing its task. It served as a guide, helping photographers understand the general direction of image processing and determine the order and necessity of various operations.

But as I continued analyzing the interactions underlying this system, I gradually began to realize that I had stumbled upon something far more significant than simply a “color correction assistant.”

The Photography Triangle allowed me to coherently place within itself compositional techniques, elements of the creative process, ideas and philosophical concepts. It could be applied not only for analyzing photographs, but beyond their bounds.

At its core lies a very simple triangular system, the work with which most resembles using a compass.

A compass cannot find your global coordinates or give you a detailed understanding of the terrain. But it will help you get oriented and on this basis independently seek the path you need.

The Photography Triangle is capable of becoming for you an analog of a compass, helping you navigate the world of photography, while not giving, by itself, answers to all questions. It was neither invented by me nor taken as a given.

On the pages of this book, I invite you to join me, step by step, to find its basic elements, the ways they interact, and to draw the remarkable conclusions to which they will lead us in the end.

But there is also another, personal reason that prompted me to write this book.

Photography gave me a language with which I could not only convey my feelings to others, but also better understand them myself. But the more deeply I immersed myself in the study of composition, color correction, and shooting rules, the more I began to lose interest in photography itself. Instead of the “magic of the moment,” I began to see in photography only a lifeless construction of pixels.

And then I tried to apply the Photography Triangle for something it was never originally intended for—to analyze my own position in the field of photography and to assess where I wanted to go.

Through this, I was able to reinvent myself as a photographer, find new meanings, and understand where to go next.

Perhaps this book will help some of you look at your shooting and color correction process in a new light. And if it inspires even one person to pick up their camera again, I will consider it a success.
